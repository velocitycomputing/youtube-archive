## Summary

Dr. Eric Schmidt discussed how Ukraine's ongoing war serves as a laboratory for AI-integrated future warfare, where transparent battlefields with drone swarms have made traditional military equipment obsolete and frozen conventional combat through constant aerial surveillance and targeted strikes. He detailed China's rapid AI progress—now within six months of US capabilities despite hardware restrictions—and emphasized that open-source Chinese models pose global security risks unlike proprietary US systems. Schmidt argued that traditional software programming is ending: developers now spend their time directing AI systems (Claude, Gemini) rather than writing code line-by-line, representing a fundamental shift in how software gets built. He also covered the massive infrastructure demands of AI (gigawatt-scale data centers costing ~$50 billion each), noting that capital availability—not energy—is the real limiting factor, and stressed the critical need for international agreements on controlling powerful AI models to prevent misuse by bad actors.

For individuals and institutions, the immediate actions are: young people should stop learning traditional programming and instead focus on managing and directing AI tools, as top practitioners will command 10x economic returns; companies must immediately shift to AI-assisted development or fall behind competitors; the military must accelerate procurement away from large vulnerable systems toward automated swarm-based robotics and build its own classified AI data centers; and the US government must pursue multi-stakeholder international agreements to govern open-source AI model distribution, while generating training data through drone competitions on military ranges to develop predictive battlefield capabilities before adversaries do.
