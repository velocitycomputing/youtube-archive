## Summary

Vegas experienced a 7.5% drop in visitors in 2025—its largest decline outside of the pandemic—prompting headlines declaring the city dead. However, the real story isn't that Vegas is failing, but that it's deliberately transforming into a luxury destination for the wealthy. Casino corporations (MGM and Caesars control ~70% of the Strip) have shifted their business model from mass-market appeal to high-value customers, eliminating cheap attractions and multiplying fees: resort fees, parking, $14 bottled water, $75+ minibars, and service charges at every turn. This reflects a broader "K-shaped economy" where the richest 10% drive half of all consumer spending. Vegas epitomizes this strategy through Formula 1, which blocks the Strip for four months, covers famous attractions like the Bellagio fountains, and caters exclusively to wealthy international tourists. Meanwhile, workers like Uber drivers, chefs, and small business operators face declining income, and the middle-class families who once fueled the city's appeal are being priced out and unwelcome.

**For visitors:** Unless you have substantial disposable income, reconsider Vegas as a destination—costs are prohibitive and the experience is explicitly not designed for you. Downtown Vegas offers an alternative with fewer corporate fees and more authentic local culture. For anyone evaluating Vegas's future, understand that while gaming revenue remains up due to ultra-high-stakes gamblers, this business model depends entirely on concentrated wealth spending. Business leaders acknowledge this may not be sustainable long-term, which suggests Vegas could continue deteriorating as a destination if middle-class visitors and workers continue to abandon it.
