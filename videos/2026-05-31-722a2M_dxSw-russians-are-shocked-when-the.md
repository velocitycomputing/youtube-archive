## Summary

Elvira Bary, a Russian author, discusses her recently completed novel—unpublishable in Russia due to censorship laws—which exposes how authoritarian elites maintain power through a "fungi structure" of corruption and complicity. Her book tells the story of a wealthy Russian woman whose daughter chooses to fight for Ukraine rather than accept a privileged life funded by state theft, illustrating the identity crisis facing Russian society. Bary emphasizes that the real problem is not that Russians are "brainwashed" but that they've been systematically "infantilized"—stripped of agency and responsibility. Most ordinary Russians oppose the war, but structural constraints (lack of education, internet censorship, economic dependence on the state, fear of imprisonment) leave them feeling powerless. Pensioners, schoolteachers, and youth trapped in a propaganda environment have limited capacity to resist, even if they consciously disagree with the regime.

For Western audiences, Bary argues the West must stop using blanket hostility toward all Russians and instead provide a clear path forward for those rejecting the regime's crimes. Distribution of her book to Russian speakers offers concrete support for counter-messaging. The West should learn from Ukraine's successful identity reconstruction through local pride and democratic values, then help Russians envision a similar future where they're recognized for creativity and contribution rather than military service. Avoid simplistic categorizations of "good" versus "bad" Russians; instead, develop objective criteria for assessing which interactions with Russian individuals or institutions advance meaningful change.
