## Summary

Kyle Kinane performs extended rants about the formulaic absurdity of Fast & Furious action movies, describing how governments inexplicably recruit car thieves to stop terrorists hiding nuclear codes in a bright orange Lamborghini. He mocks the film's casting (Ludacris apparently unaware he's in the movies, Tyrese solving engineering problems with whiteboard scribbles) and glorifies Vin Diesel mindlessly shooting civilians before driving a car off a building. His central complaint: the breaking point of disbelief isn't flying Lamborghinis—it's the injured, traumatized hero expected to perform sexually immediately after nearly dying. He pivots to adopting a feral 16-year-old neighborhood cat, which the vet claims requires $70 prescription food for its thyroid condition. The cat kills and swallows a bird whole, prompting him to call back; the vet admits cheap chicken baby food works equally well. This leaves his Oregon home with a recycling bin overflowing only with baby food jars and liquor bottles every week for three years, making neighbors assume he's raising an unseen child while being an alcoholic.

If you buy large quantities of anything unusual (baby food, alcohol), expect neighbors to notice and form conclusions—your trash and recycling publicly advertise your household. For anyone frustrated by action movie logic, this special validates that suspension of disbelief has limits, and those limits are usually sexual.
