## Summary

Kaliningrad, Russia's Baltic exclave housing 1 million civilians and a major military garrison, has been systematically blockaded by NATO members since Ukraine's 2022 invasion. Poland sealed border crossings (reducing passage to five vehicles per hour with 8-12 hour waits), closed Russian intelligence-gathering consulates, and built the Eastern Shield fortress across 700 km of border. Lithuania halted railway petroleum transit entirely (cutting 371,000 tons annually to zero), threatened rail removal, and all Baltic states disconnected from the Soviet electrical grid, forcing Kaliningrad onto expensive, weather-dependent floating LNG terminals. Only the sea route remains—four times costlier than previous methods, NATO-controlled, insufficient in capacity, and exposed to attack like Ukraine proved possible in the Black Sea.

Russia now faces an impossible equation: maintaining Kaliningrad drains resources desperately needed in Ukraine, where monthly losses (35,000 soldiers) exceed mobilization (27,000), while nuclear threats have lost credibility since Russia cannot risk opening a second front. The blockade works through economic strangulation rather than military force, demonstrating NATO's coordinated containment strategy. Abandoning Kaliningrad is politically unacceptable to Moscow (symbolizing WWII victory and providing ice-free Baltic access), but sustaining it indefinitely at current costs may become impossible—forcing Russia toward an eventual choice between bleeding resources to hold it or accepting the geopolitical loss of its most strategic position in Europe.
