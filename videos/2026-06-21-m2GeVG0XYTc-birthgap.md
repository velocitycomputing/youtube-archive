## Summary

**What Was Discussed:**
The documentary traces how expert predictions of catastrophic overpopulation in the 1970s have been inverted by an unprecedented global birth-rate crisis. Since the early 1970s—triggered by Japan's oil shock—childlessness has exploded across developed nations, not smaller families. The key finding: in Japan, the proportion of women having no children jumped from 5% (1974) to 33% (1990), with family sizes remaining unchanged. Today, 75% of the global population lives below the replacement level (2 children per woman), creating "birth gaps"—deficits between newborns and future retirees. South Korea leads at 69% birth gap, Italy/Germany at 60%, the US at 29% in New England. The trend has spread systematically from Europe and Japan to Latin America, driven by economic uncertainty, work-life incompatibility, and delayed expectations about when life feels "ready" for children.

**What's Actionable:**
The US has a critical advantage: immigration has delayed the birth-rate collapse, giving America a real-time preview of Europe and Japan's unsustainable futures. Urgent policy levers exist: restructure work to allow career + parenthood compatibility (paid parental leave alone proved insufficient in Denmark), make childcare/education affordable, and create visible role models of working parents. The psychological trigger was economic pessimism after 1973; preventing future shocks requires stabilizing young people's confidence in their futures. Most critically, this issue needs immediate public and political attention before the cascade hits workforce capacity and pension systems—unlike Europe, America still has time to act.
