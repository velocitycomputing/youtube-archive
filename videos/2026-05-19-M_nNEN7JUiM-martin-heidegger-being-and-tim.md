## Summary

Heidegger's *Being and Time* rejects Enlightenment philosophy's subject-object split, arguing that being (*Dasein*) precedes thought and is fundamentally "being-in-the-world"—inseparable from our surroundings. He describes how we live through tools and concepts in a "ready-to-hand" state where they disappear from consciousness until they break, and how society traps us in "everydayness," where we adopt preordained roles and social expectations ("fallenness"). Authenticity emerges only when we confront our finite existence, mortality (*Das Nichts*), and the anxiety this produces. Our temporal structure—reaching toward the future while carrying the past—shapes our present reality; understanding this temporal relationship reveals what it means to truly be human.

To live more authentically, periodically step back from work, routines, and social obligations to examine your biases and recognize your unity with nature, other beings, and infinite perspectives. Embrace vulnerability by acknowledging your finitude and the specialized nature of human existence. Make choices because you genuinely want to rather than defaulting to social norms. Take time in solitude—walks in nature or disconnection from the mundane—to perceive your fundamental connection to everything around you and escape the roboticism of everyday life.
