## Summary

Nick V, a Nashville studio musician and producer, argues that moving to Nashville in 2026 is still viable but requires a fundamentally different skill set than 20 years ago. Success now demands high-level instrument competency combined with strong social skills, financial management, and versatility across multiple musical genres—not specialized expertise in one area. He outlines the reality of different income paths: touring musicians earn $300–500 per show (or $75–100k+ annually on salary with side work), Broadway players doing doubles and triples can exceed six figures, and studio session players making 75% of income on acoustic guitar through custom projects with songwriters and artists. He emphasizes that Nashville's expensive cost of living (housing $500k+, coffee $10, burgers $20) requires realistic income expectations. He highlights instrument-specific requirements: guitarists must excel on acoustic and develop producer/bandleader skills; drummers need home recording setups and Ableton proficiency; keyboardists should own real pianos and B3 organs; bassists should develop arranging and mixing capabilities.

To succeed, aspiring Nashville musicians should arrive already seasoned with extensive repertoire and professional gear, then aggressively network by being personable and performing exceptionally at every gig—small first jobs lead exponentially to larger opportunities as musicians build reputation within interconnected professional circles. Practical action steps include: developing multiple income streams as your career evolves (studio, live, teaching, producing); investing in home recording infrastructure to build a local client base; mastering your instrument's primary role on the Nashville market (acoustic guitar dominates, drums need Ableton skills); hiring an accountant to manage finances; maintaining professionalism and integrity (avoiding drugs, drama, and political posts that "cut your income in half"). Success is achievable for those with strong work ethic, people skills, business acumen, and adaptability—but it requires treating music as both an art and a business.
