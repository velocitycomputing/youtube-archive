## Summary

The documentary examines the devastating March 11, 2011 magnitude 9.0 earthquake and resulting tsunami in northeastern Japan, which killed thousands and destroyed multiple coastal cities including Rikuzentakata, Kesennuma, Ofunato, and Otsuchi. It documents how geographic and oceanographic factors amplified the disaster—particularly deep, funnel-shaped bays that focused tsunami energy, causing waves to exceed 40 meters in height. The film features the story of Miki Endo, a disaster prevention officer in Minamisanriku who continuously broadcast evacuation warnings from her office building until it was engulfed, likely saving thousands before losing her own life.

The documentary illustrates reconstruction strategies now implemented across affected communities: artificially elevating terrain by up to 15 meters before rebuilding, constructing massive new breakwaters and seawalls, and relocating residential areas inland to higher ground. Viewers learn that geographic vulnerability assessment—understanding local bay topography, seabed profiles, and tectonic positioning—is critical for disaster preparedness, and that comprehensive public evacuation warning systems and childhood tsunami education significantly improve survival rates. These lessons apply to any coastal region at risk of seismic activity or tsunamis.
