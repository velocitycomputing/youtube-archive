## Summary

A tick stick (also called a spile stick or wiggle stick) is a carpentry tool for transferring irregular, complex shapes onto any material—steel, plywood, glass—with precision on the first try. The process uses a thin cardboard template called a "storyboard" placed over the irregular shape you need to match; a specially-shaped stick with crisp edges is then traced at multiple numbered contact points on this template. Those numbered points are transferred to the actual material being cut, the tick stick is repositioned to align all marked points, and you cut along the traced outline. The speaker learned this method from blacksmith Dean Mook and successfully used it for fitting steel around a fireplace opening and for roof sheeting where conventional tools couldn't work.

Use a tick stick whenever you face a one-shot fabrication problem with irregular shapes—create a thin cardboard storyboard over your target shape, trace your tick stick at sequential numbered points, transfer marks to your material, verify alignment, then cut. This works for any material and project size; practice with a scrap "try piece" first if precision matters. It's a low-cost, proven technique from boat building that eliminates the guesswork of fitting custom pieces to bizarre angles and curves.
