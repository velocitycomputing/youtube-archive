## Summary

Ukraine is systematically targeting supply routes to Crimea with drone strikes, causing severe fuel and food shortages during peak tourist season. By concentrating this pain in Russia's premier vacation destination—where over 6 million Russians vacation annually—Ukraine has created a psychological warfare effect far more powerful than dispersing strikes across all of Russia. The Kerch Strait Bridge connecting Crimea to mainland Russia is damaged and unusable for heavy vehicles due to Ukrainian strikes; Russia's air defenses cannot respond effectively without exposing Moscow and St. Petersburg; and civilian insurance companies have stopped covering deliveries to the war zone, making it impossible for Russia to resupply Crimea at scale. This campaign forces millions of ordinary Russians to experience the war's real consequences—5-10 hour fuel lines, rationed pasta purchases, and destroyed infrastructure—rather than feeling distant economic effects.

The actionable insight is understanding how concentrated pressure on high-visibility targets creates disproportionate psychological and political impact compared to distributed harm. If you're analyzing conflict strategy, geopolitics, or how public opinion shifts during wars, this demonstrates that inflicting pain where civilians personally experience it (via vacations, family connections, familiar spaces) generates far greater pressure for policy change than equivalent suffering spread thinly across an entire population.
