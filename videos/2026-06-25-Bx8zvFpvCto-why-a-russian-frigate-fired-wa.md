## Summary

The video explains why a Russian frigate fired warning shots at a British yacht in the English Channel by examining how warships defend themselves against asymmetric threats. Warships operate under rigid standardized procedures because they are extremely vulnerable—one direct hit can sink the ship and kill the entire crew—and must coordinate complex operations under time pressure with no room for improvisation. The creator outlines a typical escalation procedure starting at 2 nautical miles (increasing readiness), then VHF radio hailing at 1 nautical mile, horns and lights at 0.5 nautical miles, warning shots in the air at 0.3 nautical miles, and shots across the bow at 0.2 nautical miles. The Russian frigate fired at 400-500m, consistent with this protocol. The incident is understandable given that Russia is at war with Ukraine and views every approaching vessel as a potential threat, especially when the frigate was reportedly drifting and low on fuel, unable to maneuver away.

If you operate a yacht or vessel in the English Channel, Baltic Sea, Danish Straits, or areas with Russian warship presence, maintain significant distance from them. What feels like adequate clearance to you (400-500m) is dangerously close to a warship crew and will trigger armed defensive procedures. Sailors manning machine guns are nervous and prepared to engage perceived threats, so the practical action is to avoid approaching Russian warships entirely.
