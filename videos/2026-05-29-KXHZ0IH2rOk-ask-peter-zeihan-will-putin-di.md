## Summary

Peter Zeihan discusses why Putin is unlikely to be assassinated and explains Russia's war as driven by geographic strategy and demographic collapse. Russia's flat terrain requires control of geographical chokepoints—a goal pursued since 1982 when the KGB intelligence apparatus took over. This clique, including Putin, has systematically consolidated power and purged rivals over 23 years, leaving approximately 130 people at the top level who all share an identical worldview. Simultaneously, Russia's demographics are catastrophic: the education system collapsed in 1986, meaning the youngest people with full technical training are now 60 years old and maintaining nuclear missiles, railways, and energy infrastructure. With 1.3 million Russians under 35 fleeing since the war began (mostly draft-dodging men), Russia is rapidly losing its fighting-age population—only 8 million men in their 20s remain. The Russian leadership views this war as existential: they must secure those geographic barriers now or lose the capacity to defend themselves forever.

There is no negotiated off-ramp for Russia because the war has already cost them strategically and economically, leaving them no acceptable peace terms that don't involve admitting war crimes and paying reparations. The only realistic scenario for ending the war is decisive Ukrainian military victory that humiliates Russia domestically, potentially triggering an uprising in Moscow similar to post-1905 or post-WWI Russian revolutions—at which point the aging technical cadre maintaining the state will have mostly died of natural causes, making recovery impossible. Even if Putin "disappears," his successor will continue the war because everyone in the 130-person decision-making circle shares his ideological commitment to controlling those geographic gaps. Expect this conflict to continue for years or decades as Russia's system slowly collapses under demographic decay and military losses, with no political pathway to peace available to Russian leadership.
