## Summary

Chili's CEO Kevin Hoffman reversed the casual dining industry's slump through operational excellence and strategic product focus. He streamlined kitchen operations—eliminating the 5-gallon pickle jug for cheaper jars, replacing two fries with one to improve heat and consistency, and redesigning the seasoning shaker to properly coat fries. The menu was pruned 25% over two years, which allowed better consistency: removing the 20% less-popular tempura chicken crispy variant enabled making 24 chicken crispers at once instead of one or two, resulting in 66% sales growth on that item. Chili's introduced the Big Smasher burger to compete with fast-food pricing, marketed the Triple Dipper aggressively on social media (driving 70% sales growth and 14% of total sales), and deployed a "barbell strategy" balancing affordable items like a $6 margarita with premium options to sustain profit margins. The company's overall sales increased 50% in three years, with new customer traffic dramatically compressed return-visit timelines.

For restaurant operators or consumer-facing businesses: the actionable insight is that competing on price alone loses to competitors—instead, focus on fundamentally improving product quality, eliminating complexity from operations that prevents consistency, and leverage social media to showcase the *experience* (like cheese pulls in the Triple Dipper) rather than just the price point. Hofman's "don't get bored of the fundamentals" philosophy and his practice of conducting 16 annual listening sessions with 15-20 area managers asking "what would you change tomorrow?" provide a template for gathering frontline feedback that drives operational innovation.
