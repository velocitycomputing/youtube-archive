## Summary

Chene Bavel, a futurist specializing in AI and society, discusses why unemployment hasn't spiked despite a year of AI warnings. She explains that short-term impacts (3 years) are being overestimated by tech leaders motivated by fundraising incentives, while long-term transformation (7-10 years) is more likely—historically, 60% of jobs 80 years ago didn't exist today. Early signals already show subtle shifts: capital moving to AI-resilient companies, multilateral institutions planning for AI impacts, hardware manufacturers redesigning devices, and job postings changing in skill composition (financial analysts now direct AI rather than crunch numbers). The current stability masks an approaching shift, similar to how subprime mortgages signaled the 2008 crisis before the dramatic collapse.

If your work involves a computer, expect repricing of your labor as AI capabilities become expected, not exceptional. Start building AI skills now while you have the arbitrage opportunity—use time savings to develop new capabilities rather than just coasting. Monitor whether your current tasks will still exist in 2-3 years; the work you do will likely transform entirely rather than simply accelerate. Watch for your manager to shift from acknowledging AI usage to demanding it as baseline performance, and recognize that judgment and direction of AI systems—not just technical execution—will define valuable roles going forward.
