## Summary

Scott Fulton, a professor of healthspan and aging, argues that popular fitness advice—VO2 max training, HIIT, strength training—misses the mark because fitness and healthspan are not the same thing. He presents evidence that countries with the most fitness focus (US, UK, Australia, Canada) have the worst healthspan-to-lifespan gaps, suggesting survivor bias and data misinterpretation plague fitness messaging. Rather than pursuing elite performance, he advocates focusing on the ability to perform Activities of Daily Living: walking on varied surfaces, rising from the floor unaided, climbing stairs, carrying loads, balancing, reacting quickly, and maintaining cognitive function. He introduces five interdependent domains of functional healthspan—strength and power, cardiovascular and recovery, mobility and balance, flexibility and structure, and neuromotor and cognition—and emphasizes that healthspan is dictated by the weakest link across these domains, not excellence in any single area.

To protect your healthspan, prioritize multi-domain activities that collectively address all five functional areas: yoga, Pilates, dancing, and hiking rank highest (covering four domains), followed by tennis, pickleball, swimming, rowing, basketball, and brisk walking on varied terrain (three domains). For strength training, focus on functional movements that convert to real-life needs—Turkish get-ups, farmer carries, step-ups with load, split squats, and lunges—rather than machines that isolate single muscles. Identify your personal blind spot (the one domain you neglect) and deliberately strengthen it; cycling and running alone won't guarantee later-life function because they leave gaps in other domains. Use the five-domain framework to assess yourself regularly and choose activities that train balance, cognitive engagement, varied terrain, and reactive movement alongside traditional exercise.
