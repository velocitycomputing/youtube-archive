## Summary

Vince Gill demonstrates his ear-tuning technique at Gruhn Guitars in Nashville, explaining the principles behind tuning by listening rather than relying solely on electronic tuners. He emphasizes tuning in fifths (comparing intervals between strings), listening for "beating" or wobbling between notes to identify which string is out of tune, and using gentle physical tugs to fine-tune sharp strings. Gill explains that tuning up to pitch rather than down prevents drift, and that after establishing a baseline, you should play actual chords and adjust individual strings slightly for optimal voicing—because perfect mathematical tuning often doesn't sound best on acoustic guitars due to their inherent design compromises.

To apply this: start with one reference string, use fifths (not thirds or unison octaves) to compare intervals, and listen for the wobble between strings to find the out-of-tune one. When a string is sharp, gently tug it downward rather than reaching for a tuner; always tune up to pitch. After coarse tuning, play the chords you'll actually use and make micro-adjustments so they ring true—accept that different chord voicings may require slight retuning. Place your finger directly behind the fret (not on top of it) for accurate pitch perception, and trust your ears over electronic precision.
