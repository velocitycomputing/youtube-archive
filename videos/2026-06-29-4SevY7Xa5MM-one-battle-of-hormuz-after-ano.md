## Summary

A memorandum of understanding between the US and Iran repealed the US blockade on Iranian shipping, leading to a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz with three competing routes: an Iranian-controlled northern "toll booth" route, a US-managed southern route (Project Freedom), and a center international channel blocked by mines. Traffic peaked at roughly 60-70 ships on June 25th (about half pre-war levels), but Iranian drone and missile attacks on the Ever Lovely container ship and a crude tanker triggered US military retaliation, causing shipping to drop sharply again. The core issue is ambiguity: Iran established a Persian Gulf Strait Authority requiring ship registration, but the sanctioned entity prevents Western shipping companies from complying, leaving ships uncertain which actions provoke attacks. Fourteen mariners have been killed since February, and Iran's targeting appears designed to enforce its authority over the Strait and potentially position itself to collect future transit fees in coordination with Oman.

For shipping companies and energy markets, the immediate actionable reality is that the Strait's stability depends entirely on political negotiation, not military solutions—Iran has learned from the Houthis that 150 attacks hitting only 24 ships disrupts 10% of global trade. Current energy price stability is temporary, built on pre-conflict stockpiles now depleting, Chinese demand reduction, and Venezuelan alternatives; US refined product inventories are at record lows and finite. Shippers face rising insurance costs and ambiguous targeting criteria, making route planning a guessing game. The US-Iran negotiations supposed to resume on the nuclear issue will likely be drawn back into Strait control disputes, with the Strait itself now functioning as Iran's primary negotiating leverage against the global economy.
