## Summary

The Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant—Europe's largest with six reactors capable of powering 4 million homes—was seized by Russia in March 2022 and has since been entirely shut down. However, the facility still requires continuous cooling systems powered by external electricity; it has repeatedly lost external power during the conflict and now relies on emergency diesel generators to prevent catastrophic failure. Russia warns the situation is approaching a "point of no return," while Ukraine argues Russia illegally captured the plant and uses it as military cover. The plant, worth $25–40 billion to build today, generated billions in annual electricity revenue before the war but now consumes money in security, maintenance, and monitoring costs instead of producing it.

Even if a ceasefire occurred immediately, experts estimate it could take years to restart the facility due to maintenance concerns, staff fatigue, and grid infrastructure damage after three years of wartime operation. A major nuclear incident here could cost $250–700 billion (Chernobyl had only one reactor; Zaporizhzhia has six), potentially collapsing public confidence in nuclear energy across Europe and destabilizing energy markets during Europe's push toward nuclear expansion. The immediate actionable concern: this is no longer just a Ukrainian problem but a European economic and energy-security crisis. As long as the war continues, the risks remain uncontrollable, with cleanup liability likely falling on Ukraine and Western taxpayers if disaster strikes.
