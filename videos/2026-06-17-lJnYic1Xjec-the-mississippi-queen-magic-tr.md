## Summary

The video explains Leslie West's "major-minor pentatonic conversion" trick from "Mississippi Queen," a technique used by guitar legends from Clapton to Van Halen. By moving any pentatonic lick up three frets, you convert major pentatonic to minor pentatonic (and vice versa)—same fingering, completely different emotional color. The third note (major third vs. minor third) is what defines the tonality. West uses this conversion strategically throughout "Mississippi Queen's" solo and the same principle appears in classics like "Crossroads," "You Shook Me All Night Long," and "Tush," where artists deliberately shift between major (bright, upbeat) and minor (dark, bluesy) to control what listeners feel over each chord change.

To apply this: practice the three-fret conversion with the minor pentatonic you already know—this instantly gives you major pentatonic without learning new fingerings. Listen to the four featured songs and train your ear to spot the major-minor shifts. Then play a three-chord blues backing track and experiment: start with major pentatonic over the I chord and switch to minor over the IV and V, or try the reverse. The actionable takeaway is replacing random noodling with deliberate harmonic choices that make your playing sound intentional and professional.
