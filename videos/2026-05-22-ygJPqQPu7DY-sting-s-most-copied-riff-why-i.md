## Summary

The video breaks down why Sting's "Shape of My Heart" riff is deceptively difficult to play. The riff, co-written by guitarist Dominic Miller, is built on an F# minor descending baseline using the Andalusian Cadence chord progression, combined with Travis picking fingerstyle technique. The riff's complexity comes from layering seventh chords, suspensions (sus4), syncopation, spread triads, and anticipated bass notes. Critically, the chords are voiced as first inversions—not simple sixths as commonly believed—creating shapes like B minor over D and C# minor over E. Two chord transitions are particularly brutal: one requiring a stretch to add a high E note while keeping lower frets pressed, and another using an awkward A chord voiced as a G major shape. Dominic Miller himself often avoids these difficult fingerings in live performances, sometimes using open strings or alternate tunings.

To learn this riff, focus on understanding chord inversions rather than memorizing "sixth chords," and practice syncopated picking patterns with the bass notes hitting slightly before each chord change. For the hardest chord shapes, try using open strings (open D and A) for an approximation, or experiment with tuning the high E string up to F to barre easier chord voicings like Miller does. The bigger lesson: understanding the underlying chord theory (inversions, anticipated bass, spread triads) helps you either nail the shapes or find playable workarounds rather than struggling blindly.
