## Summary

The documentary explores Japan's "johatsu" phenomenon, where 10,000–20,000 people annually vanish intentionally from their lives. An underground industry of "Yonigaya" (disappearance agencies) facilitates this for approximately $9,000, providing comprehensive services: secret relocations, digital footprint erasure, new residences, forged documents, and employment in the informal economy. Disappeared individuals typically hide in dense urban centers like Tokyo and Osaka where anonymity is easier, or relocate to remote rural areas. The primary drivers are Japan's intense shame culture around failure, workplace pressure causing "karoshi" (death by overwork—2,959+ documented cases in 2019), consumer/business debt with no perceived escape, and relationship breakdown stigma. This phenomenon has grown since the 1960s economic boom as social standards tightened.

For viewers, recognizing the societal pressures fueling johatsu—extreme perfectionism, intolerance of failure, and inadequate mental health resources—offers perspective on Japanese culture and warning signs in any high-pressure society. If concerned about someone considering disappearance, understanding these root causes allows for more empathetic intervention focused on formal debt relief, workplace reform advocacy, and counseling rather than judgment. The phenomenon illustrates that economic development without psychological safety creates the conditions for mass voluntary disappearance.
