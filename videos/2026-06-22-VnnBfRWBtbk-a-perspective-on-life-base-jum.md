## Summary

Andy Lewis, a professional slackliner and BASE jumper, recounts his extreme sports career and philosophy on mortality. He traces slacklining's history to Korea 2,000 years ago and discusses his competitive achievements, including gold medals in 2008 and 2012. Throughout the interview, he describes increasingly risky stunts—BASE jumping off Vegas buildings (which prompted the city to criminalize the sport), free solo highlining, and experimental aerial maneuvers. A central theme is the deaths of 50+ friends and fellow athletes in extreme sports, including his hero Dean Potter, which has forced him to confront mortality directly. He argues that death is natural, not tragic, and that understanding our finite time clarifies what matters.

To live more meaningfully, Andy advocates three actionable shifts: practice intentional gratitude before difficult moments (he breathes and appreciates his health, relationships, and future before jumps); reframe change as the source of all positive things in your life, not just negative ones; and recognize that the true opposite of life is not death but *not living*—refusing to engage with the universe and pursue what matters to you. By accepting mortality and prioritizing what you love, you become more grateful for each day and more courageous in pursuing meaningful experiences.
