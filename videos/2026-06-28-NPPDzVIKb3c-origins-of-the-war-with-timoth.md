## Summary

Timothy Snyder traces Ukraine's national identity back to the 17th-century Cossack period rather than merely the 19th century, emphasizing it was grounded in deep historical awareness and founded on progressive, federalist ideals—not reactive nationalism. He describes how Ukrainian political thought survived the 20th century's traumas (Holodomor, WWII, Soviet repression) through diaspora scholarly institutions, inter-war Poland's relative openness, and even Soviet-era cultural support in the 1920s. Crucially, the Bolsheviks created the Soviet Union with a Ukrainian federal republic precisely because they recognized Ukraine's empirical national existence; this same federal structure allowed Ukraine to vote for independence in 1991 with overwhelming majorities across all regions, effectively dismantling the Soviet Union.

For readers and policymakers, the key insight is that Ukraine's post-1991 statehood was not a 20th-century invention but the reassertion of a centuries-old nation with federalist traditions—traditions that align naturally with European integration. Understanding this history challenges simplistic narratives about Russian-Ukrainian relations and explains why Ukraine's independence is rooted in lived historical reality rather than abstract political theory, making its sovereignty claims far more durable than propagandistic denials suggest. The discussion hints that 1990s policies toward Russia set conditions for later conflict, implying that post-Soviet integration strategies merit historical scrutiny.
