## Summary

Ben Eller explains why the same pitch can be called either sharp or flat (like F# versus Gb) by revealing a fundamental rule that most guitarists never learn: every scale contains all seven letters of the musical alphabet (A through G) in order, each appearing exactly once. The principle is simple—don't break the alphabet. When you follow this rule, note names become logical and organized rather than arbitrary. Scales also follow a consistent pattern: keys either use all sharps or all flats, never both mixed together. The only exception is the F#/Gb scale, which requires "imaginary" notes (like E# or Cb) to maintain the rule, which is why these keys are rarely used.

To apply this immediately: when learning any scale, verify that all seven letters appear in alphabetical sequence. If you skip a letter (like calling a note A# when it should be Bb), you've mislabeled it. Use this framework to memorize scales faster—instead of rote memorization, scales become a predictable alphabet-based system where you just add sharps or flats as needed. This transforms scales from seeming random into a logical, memorable pattern.
