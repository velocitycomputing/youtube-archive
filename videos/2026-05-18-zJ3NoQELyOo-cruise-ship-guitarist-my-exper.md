## Summary

**What Was Discussed:**
The speaker worked as a cruise ship guitarist for three years (2011–2014) across Carnival, Princess, and Celebrity cruise lines. The audition process involved a phone-based evaluation where applicants received charts and backing tracks with one hour to prepare—they performed twice and submitted video clips demonstrating various guitar styles. Reading ability is essential (80–90% of the job involves rhythm playing, but you must read single-note melodies and complex charts fluently). Key roles include show band work (structured orchestra gigs), production shows, jazz sets, big band sets, live band karaoke (sight-reading pop songs with difficult notation), and guest entertainer support (highest pressure—musicians fly in the day of rehearsal with charts and perform that evening). Travel requires a medical exam (~£300), one modified guitar, a multi-effects unit, and appropriate clothing for guest areas.

**What's Actionable:**
If you're considering this work, invest heavily in sight-reading practice—especially jazz standards, theater charts with unusual voicings, and pop songs in standard notation. Prepare one versatile guitar and compact multi-effects rig that covers rock, jazz, soul, and funk tones. Expect to handle guest entertainers ranging from patient professionals to impatient divas; the ability to interpret charts quickly and stay composed under pressure is critical. Know that live band karaoke is particularly challenging for reading (songs like "Jump" by Van Halen have awkward notation). Budget for the required medical exam before boarding, and understand that every ship join involves mandatory safety training. Research current agencies like Prosphere and network with previous cruise ship musicians to learn which guest entertainers are difficult before they arrive.
