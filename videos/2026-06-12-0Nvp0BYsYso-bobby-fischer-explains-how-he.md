## Summary

Bobby Fischer discussed how he evaluates chess positions by seeing moves in advance—typically 1-2 moves in complex positions with many variations, but 10-20 moves in forced, linear sequences. He explained that when playing multiple simultaneous opponents, he carries the positions subconsciously and retrieves them upon returning to each board. Most grandmasters can do simultaneous play, though Fischer views it as a "stunt" rather than serious chess; some players can even play 40+ opponents blindfolded, though it causes severe exhaustion. He also analyzed a famous forced mate-in-two position from the historic Morphy vs. Duke of Brunswick game, demonstrating how systematic piece placement eliminates all escape routes.

An average person can learn basic chess moves in about 20 minutes, but surviving 5-6 moves against Fischer would require 1-2 hours of foundational training (or up to 5 hours for more solid play). The key takeaway is that advanced chess vision depends on recognizing *forced lines*—positions with limited options that allow you to see deep into variations—rather than pure computational ability. Start with learning the piece movements, then focus on studying positions where moves are forced, as this builds the pattern recognition that enables looking ahead multiple moves.
