## Summary

Jeff Senn, a session musician, discusses Fender's aggressive intellectual property enforcement against guitar manufacturers for building Stratocaster-style instruments. Fender is leveraging a default EU court judgment against a Chinese counterfeiter to demand licensing fees from builders like PRS. Senn provides critical context: Leo Fender intentionally let his patents expire in 1968, making the body shape legally "generic" (the dictionary defines electric guitar with a Strat photo), and industry norms for decades allowed copying the body but prohibited copying the headstock. Fender's current owner, Servco, has no connection to Leo, and legal experts agree the strategy targets settlements from smaller companies intimidated by litigation costs rather than legitimate trademark defense.

For musicians and buyers, this creates a market choice: continue supporting Fender despite the bullying tactics, or shift purchases to competitors like PRS and boutique builders. Manufacturers facing Fender's letters should consider fighting in court using decades of established generic-status precedent rather than accepting settlement demands. Fender's strategic vulnerability is clear: younger musicians—representing the longest purchasing window—are most alienated by this approach, making this potentially "brand suicide" if they collectively redirect their loyalty and future spending to alternative brands.
