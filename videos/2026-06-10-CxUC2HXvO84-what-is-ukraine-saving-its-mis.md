## Summary

Ukraine has developed long-range strike capabilities including the Flamingo missile (3,000 km range, 1,000 kg warhead) and claims to be producing 50 missiles monthly with plans to reach 200 by January 2026. Despite high production, combat use has been limited, indicating Ukraine is stockpiling these weapons. President Zelensky announced an "eye for an eye" policy: if Russia attacks Ukrainian energy infrastructure this winter, Ukraine will target Russian power plants and critical infrastructure. This represents a strategic shift from direct warfighting to "deterrence by punishment"—using military strength to coerce Russia into avoiding specific targets rather than just responding to attacks.

The key uncertainty is whether Ukraine's stockpiled missiles are sufficient to effectively deter Russian aggression, or if Russia will proceed with its energy infrastructure campaign anyway. The strategy's success depends on whether Ukrainian attacks on Russian power grids meaningfully impact Russian public support for the war—something not yet demonstrated by the ineffective Russian attacks on Ukrainian morale. If Ukraine executes this deterrence campaign, it signals a fundamental power shift in the conflict, with Ukraine moving from defensive posture to strategic military coercion.
