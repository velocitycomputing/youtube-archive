## Summary

The Oxford expert argues that AI predictions are not facts but prescriptions—tech executives present AI futures as inevitable to manipulate behavior and secure profit, while people passively accept them due to fear and uncertainty. Predictions create systemic dangers: companies become overly conservative (hiring identical profiles, denying loans/insurance), creating collective blindness like the 2008 financial crisis. Unlike facts, predictions can't be challenged, making them powerful tools for avoiding accountability. The "turkey problem" illustrates how historical data misleads about novel futures (climate change, unprecedented AI); the dream of "AI doing boring jobs so you work less" contradicts reality—AI actually takes interesting work and people work more. Existential AI risk is marketing hype; the real danger is humans misusing AI while operating under totalitarian-like systems that dictate reality through predictions.

Develop a critical immune system by recognizing predictions as prescriptions and asking "What does this person want from me?" when you hear them. Question predictions before accepting, demand evidence, and recognize that meaningful life (education, relationships, voting) requires inconvenience—convenience isn't the highest value. Push your government and employer for public conversations about prediction ethics, currently a blind spot. Use predictions on systems/weather, not people; when applied to people, demand population-level data, not individual profiles. Most critically: reclaim your own power and don't ask experts what to do. The future is unwritten—your choices combined with your community's will determine what happens, not the predictions of those selling solutions.
