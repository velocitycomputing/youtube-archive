## Summary

Harvard exercise professor discusses how modern sedentary lifestyles create a dangerous "mismatch" with our evolutionary biology. Humans evolved as highly active hunter-gatherers (walking 10-15 km daily), but modern technology and machines have transformed us into inactive creatures like chimpanzees. The discussion explores how excessive sugar consumption creates a vicious metabolic cycle: refined carbohydrates cause rapid blood sugar spikes, triggering excess insulin production that converts sugar to fat; those fat cells then demand more sugar, driving continued overeating. The professor explains that cancer, obesity, diabetes, and other diseases are fundamentally linked to this combination of inactivity and high-sugar diets—cancer cells are "sugar hungry," excess insulin acts as a growth hormone promoting mutations, and body fat produces inflammatory molecules. He emphasizes the critical distinction between lifespan (how long you live) and healthspan (how long you're healthy), noting the average American lives 78 years but experiences only 63 years of good health.

To improve your healthspan, aim for 150 minutes of moderate exercise weekly (about 21 minutes of brisk walking daily), which reduces breast cancer risk by 30–50% and Alzheimer's risk by 30–40%. Combine both aerobic activity and strength training rather than choosing one type. Eliminate added sugars and refined carbohydrates, replacing processed foods with whole foods containing fiber—fiber slows sugar absorption and enables your body to handle carbohydrates better. Understand that exercise fundamentally changes your hormonal response to food, meaning physical activity makes the same diet metabolically safer; if you're sedentary, you cannot outeat a bad diet, but if you're active, your body handles food differently.
