## Summary

Historian Frank Dikötter challenges the dominant narrative of the Chinese Communist rise to power established by Edgar Snow's 1935 book "Red Star Over China." Using newly accessed Chinese Communist Party archives and Russian sources, Dikötter reveals that the Communist Party was established by Soviet agents in 1921, not Mao, and remained extraordinarily small—with only 40,000 members by 1936 in a population of 500 million (a smaller percentage than communist parties in Portugal, Finland, or even the United States). The Long March of 1934-35 was actually a catastrophic retreat where Mao lost 80,000 soldiers, surviving with merely 6,000. During World War II, while Japan attacked, Mao's forces avoided combat while Chiang Kai-shek bore the brunt of Japanese assault, and the communists used the opportunity to position themselves behind Japanese lines rather than truly fighting them.

The decisive factor in communist victory was not the appeal of communism but Soviet military power. The Red Army occupied Manchuria in August 1945—a territory larger than Japan—and handed it to the communists, giving them the resources and strategic position to defeat the demoralized, American-supported Nationalist forces by 1949. The actionable insight is that the 90-year-old romantic narrative of communist liberation has obscured how Stalin's intervention and military occupation were the determining factors in China's communist takeover, and this corrected historical understanding matters for accurately assessing how communism came to power globally and how official narratives shape geopolitical perception.
