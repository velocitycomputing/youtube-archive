## Summary

The discussion covered visceral fat loss mechanisms and strategies. Visceral fat accumulation is driven by insulin resistance, which creates a self-reinforcing cycle exacerbated by sleep loss, stress, and refined carbohydrates. While resistance training helps, only aerobic exercise (running, cycling, swimming) effectively reduces visceral fat by creating caloric deficit and improving metabolic capacity. Any weight-loss method—intermittent fasting, caloric restriction, or GLP-1 drugs—works because visceral fat mobilizes first during fat loss. Intermittent fasting (typically 16:8) offers additional benefits through ketosis, which enhances cognitive function, reduces anxiety via GABA production, and activates cellular repair pathways. Age and hormones significantly impact accumulation: women experience an 8–10% annual increase during perimenopause (around age 47–50) as estrogen declines, directing fat to the belly, while men see testosterone drop 1% yearly starting at 30, resulting in up to 200% visceral fat increase between ages 25–65.

To lose visceral fat, prioritize aerobic exercise (running, cycling, swimming) at vigorous intensity, not just resistance training—aim for 3–10 miles of cardio fasted if you feel good doing so. Implement intermittent fasting (16-hour fast, 8-hour eating window) to activate the metabolic switch and cellular repair; this works without calorie counting. Ensure adequate protein intake and pair calorie restriction with resistance training to preserve muscle while losing fat. Prioritize sleep, manage stress through relaxation techniques, and limit alcohol. Women should fuel if exercising more than 2 hours to avoid hormonal disruption; perimenopause-age women should be particularly aggressive about visceral fat reduction through these methods.
