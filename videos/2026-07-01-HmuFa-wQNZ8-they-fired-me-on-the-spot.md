## Summary

The speaker shares three stories of excellent musicians who lost gigs by mishandling professional expectations. First, a highly recommended musician spent excessive line-check time troubleshooting in-ear monitor settings instead of prioritizing the headlining artist's sound requirements, and was fired. Second, a session drummer spent 20 minutes trying to improve or replace drum loops a producer had created and was satisfied with, wasting expensive studio time despite the producer explicitly saying "I like what I have"—the drummer was never called back. Third, the speaker himself lost a lucrative country-tour gig after asking to sub out one September show for other work, unaware that the artist expected full-time commitment even during sparse-booking periods; skipping that commitment also disqualified him from an expected fall tour.

To keep gigs: accept that paid work means operating on someone else's timeline and priorities—roll with imperfect monitors, bad acoustics, or provided material without complaint during the limited time available. Ask clarifying questions about expectations upfront (availability, flexibility, scope of work), not after accepting. If conditions aren't what you hoped, address issues quietly with crew afterward rather than during critical setup time. Separate personal ego from professional feedback—when a client rejects your idea, they're critiquing the fit for their project, not attacking your worth as a musician. Commit fully to stated gigs rather than subbing out, or clarify flexibility before accepting.
