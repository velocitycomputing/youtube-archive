## Summary

Chef Michael Vuola showcases The 86, a historic 102-year-old West Village steakhouse with just 10 tables and 35 seats, through meticulous behind-the-scenes footage. The documentary details his obsessive approach to sourcing and preparation: 107-day aged, coffee-and-tobacco-crusted bone-in steaks, premium Japanese wagyu, and specialty cuts like the decal (spinalis) that yield only eight pieces per steer. Every corner of the cramped 200 sq ft kitchen is hyperdesigned and utilized, with careful butchery work, fresh fish prep (sardines, Dover sole, scallops), and foie gras torchon crafted for service. Midway through prep, the restaurant receives news it's been named #12 best steakhouse globally and #1 in the US on World's Best 101, validating six months of relentless execution.

If you dine at high-end steakhouses, demand transparent sourcing—know the breed, age, and origin of your meat. Seek restaurants that obsess over single details (aging specifications, dry-aged cuts, specialty suppliers like Estelle for seasonal produce) rather than broad menus. The 86's model proves that intentional scarcity (10 tables, limited covers, strategic hours starting at 4 p.m.) and consistency across every element—from knife selection to prep protocols to pre-shift alignment—create the restaurants people wait months to experience.
