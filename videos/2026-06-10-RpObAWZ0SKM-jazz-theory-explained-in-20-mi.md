## Summary

The video covers three core jazz theory topics: chord construction, chord progressions, and scales. Jazz chords extend beyond basic triads (root, third, fifth) by adding sevenths and extensions (ninths, elevenths, thirteenths) built in stacks of thirds. The three most common seventh chords are major seven, minor seven, and dominant seven—which comprise about 90% of jazz music. Chord extensions can be altered (sharpened or flattened) but are always derived from the major scale of the chord's root, regardless of chord type. The dominant chord progression is the "2-5-1," where chords are built from the second, fifth, and first scale degrees; a major 2-5-1 uses the progression D minor seven → G dominant seven → C major seven (in C). Less common is the minor 2-5-1 (only ~10% of jazz), built from harmonic minor and featuring a half-diminished chord on the two and a minor-major seven on the one. Chord voicings—different arrangements of the same chord—range from literal interpretations to sophisticated fourth and fifth voicings.

To apply this immediately: when improvising or composing over any jazz chord, use the "chordal tone plus whole-step principle." Ensure your scale contains the chord's root, third, fifth, and seventh, then add a whole step above the root, third, and fifth to fill in the remaining notes. This yields Lydian for major seven chords, Dorian for minor seven, and Lydian-Dominant for dominant seven. Recognize 2-5-1 progressions in jazz standards (the bass line characteristic is a fourth up, then a fifth down), and understand that composers sometimes shorten or modify these progressions.
