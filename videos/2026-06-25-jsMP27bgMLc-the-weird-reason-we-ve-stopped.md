## Summary

The speaker identifies five key points about experiencing the climate crisis. There's a widening gap between rising public anxiety and declining elite/media discourse, driven by defensive politics that avoid stoking electoral backlash. While citizens are increasingly anxious about climate, they're talking about it less. The speaker argues we should stop hedging about linking extreme weather to climate change—even when direct causation can't be proven, extreme weather represents the trend of what climate crisis looks like. Climate crisis and authoritarianism interact destructively, weakening democratic institutions mutually. "Thin relations"—ethical bonds to distant strangers—will erode as climate impacts force people to ignore far-off suffering. Climate projections contain vast uncertainties; models are "jagged" and can't predict social responses. Critically, Western nations contribute far more to climate crisis but face less impact, while subsaharan Africa contributes minimally but suffers most—making this fundamentally a justice issue.

Stop self-censoring about climate connections; speak plainly about extreme weather as manifestations of climate crisis without scientific hedging. Deliberately maintain ethical solidarity with distant strangers and fellow citizens to resist the corrosive collapse of shared concern. Prepare for low-probability, high-impact climate outcomes rather than banking on median projections. Recognize that your personal climate ethics cannot escape questions of global justice: as a Western citizen, you bear disproportionate responsibility for the crisis while having greater capacity to insulate yourself, which demands deliberate action and redistribution. Support broadening the climate movement beyond typical activist demographics to include people across the political spectrum.
