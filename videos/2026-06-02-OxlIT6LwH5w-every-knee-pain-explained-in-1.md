## Summary

The video diagnoses knee pain by location and provides specific causes and treatments. It covers runner's knee (front, under kneecap) from kneecap misalignment; patella and quadriceps tendonopathy (below and above kneecap) from sudden exercise increases; bursitis (swollen at front); Osgood-Schlatter disease (painful lump below knee); meniscus tears (inside knee with catching/limited range); arthritis (diffuse pain, swelling, stiffness); pes anserine bursitis (below inside joint line); IT band friction (outer knee pain); fibular joint inflammation (outside, below joint line); popliteal muscle strain (behind knee); and baker's cysts (swelling at back). The underlying cause of many knee issues is internal leg rotation, driven by foot pronation (flat feet) or weak outer glutes.

To address your knee pain: for runner's knee or IT band tightness, massage your outer thigh with your heel for 30–60 seconds daily. For tendon pain, reduce exercise intensity to 2–3 out of 10 on a pain scale and do wall sits, squats, or assisted split squats. Ice bursitis or inflamed areas; see a doctor if red or severely swollen. For meniscus tears, do gentle heel slides and straight leg raises. Address root causes by strengthening your outer glutes (stand on one leg, move your pelvis side-to-side once daily) and correcting foot pronation with orthotics or short foot exercises and calf raises. Wear supportive footwear for downhill activities and reduce high-impact exercise initially.
