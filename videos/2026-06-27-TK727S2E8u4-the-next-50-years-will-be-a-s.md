## Summary

Douthat argues that three technological forces—substitution (replacing in-person goods with inferior digital alternatives like online dating), distraction (addictive design capturing our time and attention), and obsolescence (AI rendering human work unnecessary)—are compounding a civilizational crisis of faith to produce "mass extinction" over the next 25-50 years. By extinction he means not literal annihilation but the disappearance of taken-for-granted behaviors and cultural forms: marriage, childbearing, reading, opera, even entire nations through demographic collapse. Nothing will happen naturally anymore; all preservation requires conscious choice.

What survives this bottleneck, he argues, is faith—belief that human life has cosmic purpose and meaning worth fighting for. For believers, this is good news: your values will be "selected for" precisely because they carry the intentionality and optimism to persist. Actionable takeaways: (1) make deliberate choices about what you want to preserve in your own life and community—cultural forms, family, institutions, reading—rather than drifting into default digital life; (2) recognize you're in a conflict between Christian/biblical faith and the very different faith animating Silicon Valley's AI development, and actively choose which vision of human destiny you want to build toward; (3) model to others that "choosing life"—maintaining faith in human meaning and purpose—is possible even under pressure.
