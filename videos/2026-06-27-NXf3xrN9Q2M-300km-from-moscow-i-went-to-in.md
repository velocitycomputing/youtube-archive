## Summary

Andrey visited Ostashkov, a declining provincial Russian town 300km from Moscow that has shrunk from 27,000 to 15,000 residents, featuring neglected infrastructure, low wages (~37,000 rubles/month), and abandoned buildings. The town's saving grace is Lake Seliger, one of Russia's most beautiful lakes with islands, forests, and a functioning tourism economy where basic hotels cost 6,000 rubles per night. He explored Nilova Pustyn Monastery on a lake island (notable for its cats and survival through the Soviet era), visited a local coffee shop run by a resident who restored an old tower, toured the Nikitsky Cathedral museum housed in a 1685 church, observed Soviet-era architecture and decay, and ended by visiting the source of the Volga River—Europe's longest river that begins as a tiny stream you can step across.

If you travel to this region, Lake Seliger is worth the trip: rent a boat to explore its islands and landscapes, visit Nilova Pustyn Monastery (pay 300 rubles for the observation deck), and budget around 6,000 rubles/night for accommodation. The Volga source is accessible from the area if you have time. The town itself illustrates rural Russian decline but also grassroots resilience—expect poor road conditions, especially after rain, and plan accordingly with a reliable vehicle.
