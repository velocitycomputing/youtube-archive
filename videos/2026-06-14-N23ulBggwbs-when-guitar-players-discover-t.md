## Summary

The creator investigated the "Dumble sound"—an amplifier tone he initially associated solely with John Mayer—by researching its history, interviewing amp experts, and hands-on testing. He discovered that Alexander Dumble began building custom amps in the 1960s, primarily the Overdrive Special, with variations across generations (70s, 80s, 90s models). Beyond John Mayer and Stevie Ray Vaughan, the Dumble sound is used by numerous blues-rock and rock players including Robin Ford, Larry Carlton, Joe Satriani, and others. The sound is characterized by compression, headroom, and a responsive breakup curve, with each amp tweaked individually for its player but sharing core sonic characteristics.

To experience the Dumble sound, listen to records by Robin Ford and Larry Carlton—the artists who best showcase what the amp is capable of. If you're interested in chasing this tone, consider trying a Dumble-style amp build like the Dirty Wonderland from Amplified Nation, and experiment with gain staging and dirty switch settings to understand how the amp breaks up at different levels. Rather than buying gear to match one artist's setup, explore how the Dumble sound appears across multiple genres and guitarists to better understand what makes the amp distinctive.
