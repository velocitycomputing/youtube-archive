## Summary

The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing concluded with diplomatic warmth but minimal substantive breakthroughs. While both leaders praised each other and discussed a $30 billion tariff reduction mechanism, possible Boeing purchases, and agricultural deals, no agreements emerged on Taiwan, advanced AI chips, Iran, or semiconductor exports. Xi introduced the concept of "constructive strategic stability" to create guardrails around US-China competition, signaling China's shift from negotiating from weakness to relying on deterrence and strategic resilience. Behind the diplomatic niceties, major disputes remain unresolved, and analysts view the economic outcomes as modest relative to expectations.

China's banking system conceals a brewing crisis through systematic loan forbearance—rolling over troubled debt and keeping zombie firms alive rather than recognizing bad loans. While official non-performing loan ratios stand at 1.5%, economists estimate the true figure at 10-20%, meaning trillions in unrecognized troubled assets exist. Zombie firms now represent 16% of non-financial company assets (up from 5% in 2018), trapping capital that should fuel productivity and growth. For investors and stakeholders, the actionable takeaway is to discount official Chinese financial stability metrics, stress-test exposure to Chinese banks and companies, and prepare for either sudden recognition of bad debt (which could trigger panics in weaker lenders) or prolonged stagnation as capital remains inefficiently allocated, eroding long-term growth prospects.
