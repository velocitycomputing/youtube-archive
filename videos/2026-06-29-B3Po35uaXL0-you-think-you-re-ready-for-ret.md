## Summary

Michael Kitces discusses the "end of history illusion"—the cognitive bias that prevents people from accurately imagining their future retirement lifestyle, causing them to underestimate how their interests and priorities will evolve. He explains that people often retire "from" something (escaping a job they hate) rather than retiring "to" something (moving toward a purposeful vision), and that those retiring toward something fare significantly better. Kitces then addresses the 4% withdrawal rule, clarifying that it was designed for worst-case historical scenarios, meaning the median safe withdrawal rate is actually 6–6.5%, and that people who are willing to adapt their spending during downturns can operate with higher success rates than traditional models suggest.

To prepare for retirement, practice your intended lifestyle before fully committing: rent before buying a retirement home, try new activities before joining clubs or committing to patterns like daily golf. Start building your social networks and relationships now—invest in friendships as actively as your career—since isolation is a major factor in poor retirement outcomes. Rather than locking in a fixed withdrawal rate, use a "ratcheting strategy" where you start with conservative spending and gradually increase it when your portfolio performs well, allowing flexibility without forced retrenchment. Finally, recognize that you don't need to solve retirement perfectly upfront; if markets decline, you can adjust spending downward gradually, so a 60% "success rate" may be acceptable if you're willing to adapt rather than treating it as a binary outcome.
