## Summary

Kofu is Japan's smallest town in Tottori prefecture with a population of 2,500 and a decades-long decline, yet maintains functional community infrastructure through creative adaptation. The filmmaker documented a week exploring the town's geography—Mt. Daisen, rice terraces, consolidated schools, and clustered homes—and its services, including a mobile grocery truck that delivers goods directly to residents' homes. The town has repurposed closed schools into coffee roasters and community centers (40 total), operates a combined taxi-bus system for younger students, and maintains an economically sustainable model based on hydroelectric power, a Suntory water factory, and rice farming. The traditional town center is hollowing out as businesses relocate to the highway interchange for easier vehicle access and parking.

If you're considering relocation, Kofu offers concrete incentives: trial rental homes (30,000 yen/month), relocation subsidies for moving from Tokyo or renovating abandoned homes, 500,000 yen childbirth support (covering hospital costs), and business setup subsidies—though most require committing to 5 years residency. The relocation nonprofit can arrange multi-week trial stays across different Tottori towns to help you choose, and houses include appliances and utilities already in place, reducing moving friction. Visitors can enjoy Mt. Daisen's scenery freely, though spending opportunities in town are limited.
