## Summary

**Heart of Darkness**, Joseph Conrad's 1899 novella, is a foundational work of modernist literature that depicts a steamboat journey up the Congo River and has become the preeminent cultural representation of Western imperialism in Africa. The episode traces Conrad's actual biography—he was born Józef Teodor Konrad Korzeniowski, a Polish nobleman who became a merchant sailor, and in 1890 he joined the Société Anonyme Belge du Haut Congo and traveled upriver, where he witnessed violence, brutality, and colonial exploitation. He encountered the Irish patriot Roger Casement, contracted dysentery, and became disillusioned with the "civilizing mission," later calling the Congo Free State "a gigantic and obscene beast." Conrad wrote the novella in 1898–1899, drawing loosely on a French agent named Georgette Klene as inspiration for the character Kurtz, and the finished work profoundly influenced modernist literature and became a reference point for understanding imperialism.

The book's influence spans literature, film, and cultural criticism: T.S. Eliot quoted it in *The Wasteland*, Francis Ford Coppola adapted it as *Apocalypse Now* (1979), transposing it to the Vietnam War, and it has inspired works from video games to scholarly debates about the Western canon. For readers, actionable value lies in reading *Heart of Darkness* itself as a masterwork of English prose written in Conrad's third language and as a critical examination of imperialism; watching *Apocalypse Now* to see how the themes translate to a modern conflict; and engaging with ongoing scholarly arguments about whether the novella critiques or perpetuates racist imperialist ideology—a debate central to 21st-century literary canon discussions.
