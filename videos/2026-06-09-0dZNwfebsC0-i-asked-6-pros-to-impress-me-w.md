## Summary

The video showcases six professional chefs demonstrating distinct steak cooking techniques. Max the Meat Guy prepared a bone-in ribeye over charcoal with smoked compound butter infused with bacon, garlic, and herbs. Nick used the deep-fry method in cast iron with algae oil to achieve maximum crust on American Wagyu hanger steak, topped with sea urchin butter. A third chef created a charred scallion chimichurri to accompany a thick-cut steak seared in cast iron and finished in the oven. Chef Hiro made beef tataki—a Japanese preparation with barely-seared New York strip sliced thin and served raw with ponzu sauce, sliced onions, and scallions. Chef Brian Sao cooked Brazilian picana (a tender, fatty cut) Korean barbecue-style, serving it wrapped in perilla leaves with sticky rice, kimchi, pickled daikon, and Korean pear-scallion salad with samjang sauce. The sixth chef, Frenchie, prepared hand-carved tomahawk steaks.

Apply these actionable techniques: season steaks generously with kosher salt without fear of over-salting; use dry brining (salting in advance) to build flavor. Create compound butters with add-ins like smoked ingredients, sea urchin, or herbs for finishing. For cast iron searing, use abundant high-smoke-point oil (like algae oil) and press the steak down with weights to maximize crust. Always slice against the grain for tenderness. Explore different cuts beyond ribeye—try hanger steak or picana. Consider cultural variations like Japanese tataki (quick sear, mostly raw) or Korean BBQ service with sides. Let steaks rest adequately after cooking to retain juices, and finish thicker steaks in the oven after initial searing rather than cooking entirely on heat.
