## Summary

A major UK Biobank study of 313,000 adults found that people with the worst sleep patterns had a 76% higher risk of developing dementia and more than double the risk of vascular dementia compared to good sleepers. The research identified seven sleep factors that matter: duration (7-9 hours), falling asleep at a reasonable hour, no insomnia, no snoring, no excessive daytime sleepiness, no need to nap, and waking refreshed. The mechanism: during deep sleep, the brain's glymphatic system clears toxic proteins (amyloid beta and tau) associated with Alzheimer's disease. When sleep is poor or insufficient, these proteins accumulate over years and decades, forming plaques and tangles that damage brain cells. Poor sleep also ages the brain physically—one additional point drop in sleep quality adds six months to your brain's apparent age on MRI scans. Early signs of cognitive decline appear in your 50s-60s as subtle changes: words not coming to mind, forgetting why you entered a room, difficulty planning, repeated questions, and mood changes.

To protect your brain: establish a fixed wake time every day (including weekends), get outdoor natural light within 30 minutes of waking for at least 10 minutes, cut caffeine by 1-2pm since it has a 5-6 hour half-life, keep your bedroom cool (16-18°C) and dark without your phone, avoid alcohol especially near bedtime, exercise in morning or early afternoon only, and do a brief brain dump of worries before bed. The most critical change for most people is simply going to bed an hour earlier—closing apps that keep you scrolling until midnight and moving from 6 hours to 7-8 hours of sleep. If you snore, wake unrefreshed despite adequate sleep, or have daytime headaches, see a GP about sleep apnea, which is easily treatable once diagnosed.
