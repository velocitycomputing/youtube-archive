## Summary

The video explains that arm weakness after 60 results from sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss), which accelerates significantly between ages 60-80. However, research shows resistance training can reverse this decline at any age—even people in their 80s and 90s gain comparable muscle to those in their 50s and 60s. Rather than isolation exercises like bicep curls, the presenter recommends three compound movements: dumbbell rows (strengthening arms, shoulders, and back), curl-twist-press combinations (targeting biceps, triceps, deltoids, and rotator cuff), and pushing exercises with multiple difficulty progressions (wall press, chair press, knee push-ups, or full push-ups).

Perform this routine 2-3 times per week for 20 minutes per session: dumbbell rows (8-15 reps, 3 sets per arm), curl-twist-press movements (8-15 reps, 3 sets at a slow 3-second tempo), and one pressing variation (8-15 reps, 3 sets). Begin with the wall press if you're a beginner and progress to harder variations as you build strength. Choose whichever pressing variation allows you to complete 8-15 quality repetitions—once you can do 15+ easily, move to the next harder variation.
