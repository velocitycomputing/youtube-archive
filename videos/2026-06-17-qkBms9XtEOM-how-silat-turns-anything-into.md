## Summary

Mali, a renowned Silat instructor from Brunei, demonstrated how Silat weaponizes everyday objects and transforms martial responses through precise body mechanics. The session covered using scarves (sarongs) as flexible weapons—folded and held without wrist rolling to maintain control—enhanced traditionally with water or weights like pebbles or fish hooks to increase impact on targeted body areas. Central to Silat is off-balancing through sinking and proper structure rather than raw force; techniques include neck-breaking methods using spinal manipulation (not choking), scarf-based grappling and striking, and knife defense entries from multiple angles (inside/outside, above/below). Mali emphasized that Silat's effectiveness depends on reading an attacker's energy and intention, with proper technique requiring full body engagement, not isolated hand movements, and that traditional Brunei Silat remains upright-focused due to practitioners originally fighting on water bridges.

To learn Silat, contact regional group leaders or visit Silufan Worldwide on Facebook to find seminars and information in your country (SSD USA for the United States, other organizations across Europe). Attending in-person seminars is critical—Mali stressed that feeling the energy transfer and instructor guidance matters far more than watching videos. Once you understand the fundamentals and principles in person, rewatching videos will reveal techniques in a new light. Practice from day one with correct structure and committed intention, not casually, to develop the sensitivity needed to read and intercept an opponent's movements before they execute.
