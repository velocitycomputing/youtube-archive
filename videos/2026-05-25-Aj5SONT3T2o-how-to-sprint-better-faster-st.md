## Summary

Stuart McMillan, a world-renowned sprints coach, demonstrated how sprinting differs fundamentally from jogging: sprinting happens in front of the center of mass with an expressive, tall posture using the entire body, while jogging is a lower-body activity that happens behind center mass with a compact posture. Key technical points include landing with a stiff, flat foot (like punching a heavy bag), pulling the thigh forward rather than reaching, and generating primarily vertical force instead of horizontal force. The warm-up emphasized training the spine and hips through specific patterns: skipping instead of jogging, dynamic mobility work (skip-skip-drop lunges), rotational exercises combining hip extension with spinal rotation, leg swings in multiple planes, and progressive stride work building from 50% intensity. McMillan stressed that coordination and rhythm are more important than power or strength, and that sprinting engages the entire body including arm swing and shoulder movement.

To sprint better, replace jogging warm-ups with skipping and the specific progression shown: skip, stride, dribble steps, side shuffles, backwards movements, and leg swings before progressing to strides at increasing intensity. Focus on maintaining an expressive, upright posture; landing flat-footed with ankle stiffness; and pulling your thigh forward rather than pushing off behind you. Incorporate the spinal and hip mobility exercises—particularly the half-kneeling rotations, split squat hamstring pulls, and drinking-bird-to-standing transitions—to build the hip extension and rotational patterns that define efficient sprinting. Practice moving in multiple planes and at different velocities during warm-up, and remember that the goal is rhythmical, coordinated movement that activates your full body, not maximal power.
