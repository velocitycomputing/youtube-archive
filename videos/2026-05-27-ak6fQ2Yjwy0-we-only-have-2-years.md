## Summary

Anthropic's essay argues that by 2028, either the US and its allies maintain AI leadership through stronger export controls and disrupting China's distillation attacks, or the US fails to act and China catches up or overtakes America in frontier AI. The core claim is that compute (advanced chips) is the ultimate competitive advantage—currently held by America—and that China has been narrowing the gap through exploiting loopholes and large-scale model distillation. Anthropic warns that if authoritarian regimes like the CCP lead in AI, they could deploy it for mass surveillance and automated repression at unprecedented scales, fundamentally altering global power dynamics. The essay identifies four competition fronts: model intelligence, domestic adoption, global distribution, and political resilience.

The reviewer contradicts Anthropic's core argument: intelligence alone won't determine winners because cheap, efficient open-source models will likely be adopted globally over expensive frontier models, potentially giving China vast influence regardless of raw capability superiority. More critically, compute restrictions may accelerate rather than prevent China's chip development, and Chinese researchers are already closing gaps through algorithmic innovations. The actionable lesson is that US policy should not rely solely on compute export controls but instead focus on maintaining algorithmic leadership, ensuring democratic values embed in globally-adopted models, and recognizing that China's efficiency innovations—not just stolen compute—are driving its competitive advances.
