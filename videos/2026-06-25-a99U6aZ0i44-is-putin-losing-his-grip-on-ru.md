## Summary

**What Was Discussed**
BBC Russia Editor Steve Rosenberg examined Putin's grip on power and Russia's trajectory since the Ukraine invasion. Key points included widespread war fatigue among Russians—approximately two-thirds now prefer peace talks over continued conflict—and the introduction of repressive laws silencing dissent and criticism of the Kremlin. Rosenberg characterized Putin's system as "a house built by Putin, for Putin, around Putin," noting that while structural damage has occurred through four years of war and sanctions, the system remains standing despite shocks like the 2023 Wagner mutiny. The discussion traced Russia's evolution from the optimistic end of Soviet rule through Yeltsin's chaotic 1990s to Putin's early popularity, his watershed 2007 Munich speech revealing anti-Western resentment, and the current "Z Russia" of heightened repression. Rosenberg emphasized that Putin's imperial mindset—viewing Ukraine and Belarus as inseparable from a "great Russia"—drove his invasion despite waning Russian influence in former Soviet states, with NATO expanding on Russia's borders following the miscalculation.

**What's Actionable**
The transcript reveals that Russian public sentiment has fundamentally shifted: with two-thirds wanting peace talks, there exists potential leverage through understanding domestic opposition to the war. Observers should recognize that Putin's system, though damaged, remains stable for now with no immediate signs of collapse—suggesting long-term strategic planning rather than assumptions of imminent regime change. Independent Russian journalists face severe danger for speaking truth to power, making supporting press freedom efforts and international protection mechanisms critical. Finally, Putin's pattern of "winning" repeatedly before Ukraine suggests he may miscalculate again; Western policymakers should understand this isn't rational cost-benefit calculation but rather a leader convinced of his own strategic genius despite mounting evidence to the contrary.
