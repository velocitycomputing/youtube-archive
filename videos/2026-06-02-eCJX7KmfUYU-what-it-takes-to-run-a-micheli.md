## Summary

Franco, co-owner and chef of Frevo (a Michelin restaurant in NYC), walks through the restaurant's operational philosophy and current service prep. The core of Frevo's approach is constant menu innovation—changing dishes roughly once per week across four annual menu rotations through testing with purveyors. The video documents specific trials: testing Australian Wagyu beef (comparing plain vs. breadcrumb-crusted versions), developing a signature nori dish with delicate young sea bream, and crafting a three-day white pepper sauce for scallop carpaccio. Franco emphasizes balancing luxury ingredients (white truffles, caviar, Wagyu) with simpler, well-executed dishes (like a carrot-based plate) to surprise guests and avoid stagnation. The kitchen operates with 22 total staff (11 for service), built from just four people at the 2019 opening. Operational details include hand-rolling nori, lye-dipping pretzel bread, and a newly developed non-alcoholic pairing program designed to match wine-like complexity without alcohol.

For restaurateurs or serious home cooks, the actionable takeaway is Frevo's testing-before-committing model: order expensive ingredients in bulk, trial them during pre-service gatherings, and accept that failed experiments become team meals—this removes the pressure of perfection on first attempt. Franco also demonstrates ingredient selection discipline (choosing firm, fragrant white truffles, selecting delicate fish species, evaluating portion costs against menu price), the value of building a specialized team over time, and the principle that exceptional sauce and preparation technique matter more than ingredient cost alone. The non-alcoholic pairing framework (using tea, syrup, and umami elements to mirror Wagyu's earthiness and spice) offers a replicable template for beverage pairing without alcohol.
