## Summary

**What Was Discussed**

Current exercise guidelines recommending 150-300 minutes weekly of moderate intensity activity are based on outdated calorie-burning ratios rather than actual health outcomes. A new study using wrist accelerometers to measure real activity found that vigorous-intensity exercise is dramatically more effective than guidelines suggest: every 1 minute of vigorous activity (jogging, running, fast swimming, biking) replaces 4-10 minutes of moderate activity or 100-300 minutes of light activity for reducing mortality from all causes, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and cancer. The research also revealed that brief exercise "snacks"—short bursts of vigorous movement scattered throughout the day—provide massive health benefits, with women doing just 3.5 minutes daily of vigorous activity reducing cancer risk by 40%, and people accumulating 9 minutes daily seeing 40-50% lower mortality rates.

**What's Actionable**

Replace the "10,000 steps per day" goal with "10 minutes per day" of vigorous-intensity activity (elevated heart rate at ~70% or more of maximum). These 10 minutes don't need to be continuous—you can accumulate them through multiple short bursts: playing tag with children, sprinting with a dog, bodyweight squats, or fast-paced weightlifting. Achieving this target delivers 50% lower cardiovascular mortality, 50% lower all-cause mortality, and 40% lower cancer mortality, making it realistic even for people who dislike long gym sessions.
