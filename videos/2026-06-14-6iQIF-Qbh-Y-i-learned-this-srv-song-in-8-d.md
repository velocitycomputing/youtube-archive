## Summary

V learned Stevie Ray Vaughan's "Riviera Paradise" in eight days by focusing on two core concepts instead of note-for-note copying: blues triads and keystone licks. Triads revealed that the complex-looking intro was actually the same chord shapes repeated (A minor to A major progressions, dominant chords, CAGED shapes). Keystone licks are repeatable patterns that appear throughout the fretboard in various forms—in this song, SRV primarily uses them in the upper region, building from pentatonic scales and adding techniques like octaves and double stops. Understanding these underlying patterns made the song's structure visible rather than random, transforming learning from pure imitation into comprehension.

To apply this method: identify 2-3 keystone licks you like in a song, understand them deeply, then practice playing them across different positions on the fretboard and at the appropriate moments in the chord progression. Rather than memorizing every note, learn where and why each lick fits, then improvise within those constraints. This approach reduces pressure to sound identical to the original artist, makes learning more enjoyable, and helps you develop your own voice by borrowing techniques rather than copying them exactly.
