## Summary

Oz Noy discusses his path from stopping school at 15 in Israel to becoming a respected jazz guitarist in New York, shaped by early influences like Al Di Meola, Pat Metheny, and later Pat Martino. He describes moving to New York at 24, being intimidated by the virtuosos he encountered, and finding his own voice by switching from a hollow-body jazz guitar to a Stratocaster while playing with Gavin DeGraw—a pivotal choice that forced him to develop a unique style. He emphasizes the importance of carving out your own sonic territory rather than competing in the spaces occupied by heroes like Eric Johnson, Julian Lage, or Scott Henderson, each of whom owns their musical world so completely that others can't play in it without sounding like pale imitations.

For aspiring guitarists, the key takeaway is that rhythm mastery is foundational and non-negotiable—it must come before speed or technical flashiness. Practice with a metronome, study how bass and drums interact with rhythm guitar, and learn comping patterns for both jazz and funk (which Noy emphasizes are functionally identical). Apply octave displacement exercises across scales you already know, and adopt Pat Martino's mindset of approaching music like a child—with openness and exploration rather than predetermined ideas about what you "should" play.
