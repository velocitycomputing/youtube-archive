## Summary

Ukraine has strategically isolated Crimea by cutting off all supply routes—roads, rail bridges, and ferries—creating severe shortages of fuel, food, electricity, and water for Russian civilians. The Kerch Strait Bridge, the main connection to mainland Russia, now has three-hour queues of civilians fleeing the peninsula. Ukrainian military actions near the bridge and explicit warnings from Ukrainian leaders (including the Crimean Tatar leader and Defense Minister) are signaling that civilians should evacuate while they can. Ukraine is simultaneously destroying internal Crimean infrastructure—power plants, electrical substations, and rail bridges—while deliberately keeping the bridge open as an exit route, framing this as a mass eviction of occupiers from stolen Ukrainian territory rather than a siege.

The primary consequence will be an estimated 2 to 2.5 million Russian refugees flooding into Russia's most populated regions—either toward Moscow or the strategically sensitive Caucasus region—which could trigger severe internal social and political instability similar to migration crises seen in Western countries, but in a nation already at war. For those consuming this analysis, the takeaway is to recognize this as one of the war's most significant developments worth sustained attention, particularly regarding how Russia's government and society respond to this internal refugee crisis and what domestic unrest it may trigger.
