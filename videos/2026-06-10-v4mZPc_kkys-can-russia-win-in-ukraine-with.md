## Summary

Russia has dramatically increased its nuclear rhetoric since American military aid to Ukraine resumed and European leaders signaled deeper support, including statements about potentially deploying troops and allowing Ukraine to strike Russian territory. However, experts across the spectrum agree that nuclear use in Ukraine is extremely unlikely. Nuclear weapons function as a tool of last resort—the more confident a state is in conventional military victory, the less it relies on nuclear threats. Russia's recent nuclear posturing actually signals weakness and declining confidence in its conventional forces, not strength. The transcript explains two critical reasons Russia won't use nuclear weapons to win in Ukraine: domestically, Putin would face regime collapse if he had to explain to the Russian population why nuclear escalation was "necessary" when state propaganda claims the war is going well and the military is strong; and internationally, Russian nuclear use would almost certainly trigger direct NATO intervention and create an unstoppable wave of nuclear proliferation (Japan, South Korea, Saudi Arabia, Poland, Germany, and others would immediately pursue their own weapons), forcing all existing nuclear powers to unite against Russia to prevent this outcome.

For the user: treat current Russian nuclear threats as intimidation designed to discourage Western aid, not as genuine preparation for nuclear war. Continue supporting Ukraine without fear that weapons transfers will trigger nuclear escalation—Russia has too much to lose strategically. The only realistic nuclear risk emerges if Putin's regime itself faces collapse and he perceives Russian state existence as threatened, at which point all restraints disappear; monitor for signs of Russian internal destabilization rather than military posturing.
