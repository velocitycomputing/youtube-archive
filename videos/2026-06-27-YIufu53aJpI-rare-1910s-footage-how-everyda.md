## Summary

The documentary uses restored Ford film archives from 1914–1920 to show America's transformation from an isolated agrarian society to a motorized nation. Early footage depicts backbreaking farm labor, impassable muddy roads, and limited access to markets or cities. Henry Ford's Highland Park assembly line revolutionized manufacturing by producing a Model T every 10 seconds, making cars affordable for ordinary families. The film then documents World War I industrial mobilization (Liberty engines, Eagle boats, experimental tanks) and the post-war explosion of automobile adoption—farmers' wives gained independence to leave the farm, families took recreational drives, Sunday outings became a national habit, and modern infrastructure (highways, electrified farms, running water) followed automotive expansion into rural America.

For viewers today, the documentary illustrates how a single transformative technology fundamentally reshaped society's structure, economy, gender roles, and urban design within one generation. It demonstrates that Ford's success came not from isolated genius but from systematic documentation, affordable mass production, and solving real problems people faced (isolation, labor burden, limited mobility). The actionable insight is understanding how past technological disruption—creating both liberation and unforeseen challenges like traffic congestion—can inform how we navigate current disruptions from autonomous vehicles and other emerging technologies. The film also emphasizes the value of documenting how society transforms during periods of technological change for future historical understanding.
