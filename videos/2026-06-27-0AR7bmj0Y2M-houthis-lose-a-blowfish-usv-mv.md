## Summary

**What Was Discussed:**
The Houthi militant group attacked the container ship MV Pumba in the Red Sea on July 20, 2024, using a remotely-controlled unmanned surface vessel (USV) called a "blowfish." The ship's three-person armed security detachment repelled the attack using FN FAAL rifles, successfully detonating the USV before it could inflict major damage, though the explosion was smaller than expected. The incident illustrates the Houthis' escalating maritime attack campaign—using less sophisticated but increasingly numerous drones and USVs—while Israel retaliated by striking Houthi ports and oil facilities in Yemen. A U.S. Central Command general warned that military action alone is failing to deter attacks and called for a coordinated economic, diplomatic, and military "whole of government" response.

**What's Actionable for Operators:**
Shipping companies and vessel operators transiting the Red Sea should expect the Houthi threat to intensify, particularly for lower-value vessels (under $100 million) where war-risk insurance becomes economically justified for faster routes. While armed security detachments offer some protection, they are not reliable against coordinated multi-vector attacks or nighttime assaults. Operators should monitor escalating insurance rates, evaluate alternate routing around Africa despite longer transit times, and prepare for potential supply-chain disruptions and freight rate increases as more ships avoid the region. The security situation is at an inflection point with forces being redeployed and tactics reassessed—conditions that typically mean heightened unpredictability in the coming weeks.
