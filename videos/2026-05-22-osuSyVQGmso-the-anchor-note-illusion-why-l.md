## Summary

The instructor teaches the "anchor note illusion," a technique where you play one or a few stationary notes while chords change beneath them, allowing those notes to shift emotional character without moving your fingers. He explains how the same pitch functions differently depending on the chord—for example, a C note becomes a sad minor third over A minor, a stable fifth over F major, and a tense suspended fourth over G major. The core concept is based on note function: how a single pitch's musical role changes with different harmonic contexts. He demonstrates this with a C-F-C-G progression, then shows practical examples using E as an anchor note, followed by two-note (F-E, B-C) and three-note (C-D-E) phrases, each gaining emotional layers as the harmony shifts underneath.

To apply this, grab a backing track and start with one anchor note—listen intently to how its emotional color transforms with each chord change. Build simple guitar licks that intentionally land on that anchor note as a "home base" rather than frantically searching the neck for new scale shapes. Progress from mastering single-note phrasing to two-note combinations with expressive bends, then move to three-note phrases. This approach replaces scale gymnastics with harmonic awareness, letting the chord changes do the emotional work for you rather than you chasing the chords.
