## Summary

**What Was Discussed**

A prestigious literary prize accidentally awarded a story written largely by AI, prompting a deeper question about what creativity actually is. Neuroscientist Adam Green led a study analyzing 370,000+ college application essays before and after ChatGPT's November 2022 release, finding a striking paradox: after AI became available, essays used richer vocabulary and were rated as *more* creative by expert judges, yet simultaneously explored narrower ranges of ideas and became more similar to each other. The core discovery is that AI breaks the historic link between word diversity and idea originality—it can generate varied, sophisticated language while expressing homogeneous concepts because it operates differently than human brains. Where humans search for both words and ideas using the same semantic networks, AI searches for the next word token probabilistically, allowing it to disguise conceptual sameness with surface-level variety. This trick works so well that creativity experts—and even AI itself—cannot reliably detect it. The problem disproportionately affects underrepresented students because training data skews toward culturally dominant voices, pushing their authentic expressions toward mainstream homogeneity.

**What's Actionable for the User**

The danger is twofold: product-level (less diverse ideas circulating in culture) and process-level (atrophy of human creative thinking). Students using AI for essays make a Faustian bargain—sharper sentences but duller minds. If you rely on AI tools to do your thinking (writing, problem-solving, idea generation), you deprive yourself of the creative process itself, which research shows is genuinely healthy for cognitive flexibility, emotional resilience, and wellbeing. The actionable insight: watch whether you're outsourcing thinking rather than just refining output. If you can't tell whether a piece of writing is yours after AI polishes it, you've surrendered the process. As more knowledge workers lean on these tools, everyone incurs this risk. Detecting AI writing now requires computational help, not human intuition, so be skeptical of creative work you encounter—including your own if you're using AI as a thinking partner rather than a polish tool.
