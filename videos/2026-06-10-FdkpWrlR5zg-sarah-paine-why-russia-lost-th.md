## Summary

Sarah Paine examines why the Soviet Union lost the Cold War by touring multiple competing explanations: external ones attributing Soviet defeat to Reagan's military buildup, Nixon's China strategy, Carter's human rights pressure via the Helsinki Accords, and U.S. submarine threats to Soviet deterrence; and internal ones blaming the Soviet empire's collapse in Eastern Europe, Gorbachev's uncontrolled glasnost reforms triggering democratic contagion, simultaneous ethnic rebellions throughout the Soviet republics, Third World overcommitment draining resources while the economy stagnated, and catastrophic economic inefficiency from central planning that left decision-makers blind to actual productivity or consumer needs. She argues the Soviet Union "died by suicide, not murder"—through imperial overreach, economic rot accelerated by collapsing oil prices, and Gorbachev's pivotal false assumptions that history moved only toward communism and that Eastern Europe would accept "socialism with a human face" rather than reject Russian domination entirely.

For strategists, the actionable lesson is to rigorously test assumptions about how adversaries and occupied territories will behave: verify whether your opponent's economic system can actually sustain protracted competition, understand that occupied populations resent occupation regardless of ideology, and recognize that reform initiatives often escape their designer's control. Don't assume unidirectional historical progress, don't expect credit for "liberating" territory you previously subjugated, and avoid overcommitting to unprofitable Third World allies while your core system crumbles. The most effective Cold War strategies worked cumulatively—each president from Nixon onward built on predecessors' constraints imposed on the Soviets—rather than relying on single leaders, and internal contradictions within an opponent's system ultimately matter more than external military pressure alone.
