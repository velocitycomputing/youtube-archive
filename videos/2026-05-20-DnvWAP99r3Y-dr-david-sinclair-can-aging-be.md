## Summary

Dr. David Sinclair, a Harvard longevity researcher, presented his "information theory of aging," explaining that aging occurs when cells lose their epigenetic identity—the control systems that tell cells which genes to activate. He shared breakthroughs from his lab: a technology using three genes can reverse cellular aging by approximately 75% in tests, and mice treated with the approach achieved 100% lifespan extension. Human trials are beginning soon, starting with treating blindness in the eye. Sinclair's research shows the same reversal genes can treat multiple diseases (Alzheimer's, cancer, heart disease, ALS, multiple sclerosis) because aging is the underlying driver. He predicts a pill form of the treatment within 10 years and believes people could potentially live into the 22nd century.

To slow aging now, avoid known accelerators: smoking, processed foods, excessive alcohol, frequent flying, and loud noise—all increase cellular stress and epigenetic damage. More importantly, the key actionable insight is that aging is no longer inevitable: within a decade, pharmaceutical treatments could reverse biological age. Focus on maintaining reasonable health habits now to survive long enough to benefit from emerging longevity treatments, rather than accepting aging as unavoidable decline.
