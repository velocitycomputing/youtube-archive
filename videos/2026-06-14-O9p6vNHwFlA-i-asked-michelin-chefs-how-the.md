## Summary

Four Michelin-trained chefs demonstrated distinct chicken thigh preparations: Chef India Davis made South African peri-peri fried chicken by seasoning buttermilk with spices, coating in a spiced flour mixture, and frying at 350°F; Chef Tatsuono showed Japanese yakitori using boneless thigh pieces grilled on binchotan charcoal and repeatedly dipped in a 2:2:1 mirin-soy-brown sugar sauce; Chef Paul Carmichael prepared Caribbean calypso chicken by searing heavily salted thighs, then braising in a rich sauce with red wine, guava jelly, ginger, and cashews at 350°F; and Chef Nils Noren made Harlem-style fried chicken by brining for 3 days, coating in an 8-spice flour blend, letting it sit overnight, then double-frying at 360°F and 380°F for maximum crispness.

To replicate these at home: brine chicken thighs for 3 days if prioritizing juiciness, season buttermilk or coat with spiced flour (at least 4–8 spices including paprika, cumin, and ginger), and let coated pieces rest overnight in the fridge before frying. For yakitori, keep skin on, skewer boneless pieces, and grill over charcoal while alternating between drying the exterior and soaking in sauce. For braised versions, sear skin-side down first to render fat, then braise in wine or stock with aromatics at 350°F. For maximum crispness, use a two-stage fry (10–12 minutes at lower heat, then 1 minute at higher heat) rather than single-stage frying.
