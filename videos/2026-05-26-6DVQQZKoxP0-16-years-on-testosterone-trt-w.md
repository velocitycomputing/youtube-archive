## Summary

Dr. Ty Vincent, a physician who has been on testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) for 16 years and treated ~1,000 men, discusses how testosterone deficiency causes fatigue, low mood, poor motivation, erectile dysfunction, and joint pain—often incorrectly treated with multiple medications instead. He shares his own experience: after testing revealed only 350 ng/dL total testosterone at age 35 (dropping further to 211 after exercise), he started TRT and experienced dramatic improvements in energy, mood, motivation, and overall well-being. The key discovery he emphasizes is that problems attributed to testosterone usually stem from excessively high estrogen levels (above 100 ng/dL in men), not testosterone itself, causing emotional changes and mental disturbances that can be managed with aromatase inhibitors. He also debunks myths about fertility and anger, noting that high DHT causes hair loss and acne, and that HCG injections can maintain fertility while on TRT. His teenage sons exhibited similar low testosterone despite appearing healthy, became lethargic and unmotivated at 16, and transformed after starting TRT.

**Actionable steps:** Get testosterone tested if experiencing fatigue, depression, low motivation, or erectile dysfunction—particularly important at age 16 and beyond. If starting TRT, require monitoring of estradiol, estrone, and DHT levels 3–4 weeks after beginning treatment, and use aromatase inhibitors to keep estrogen below 100 ng/dL. Target total testosterone above 1,500–2,000 ng/dL for optimal results. If concerned about fertility, combine TRT with HCG injections. Parents of lethargic teenage boys sleeping excessively should get testosterone tested before assuming it's behavioral.
