## Summary

Russia is relocating air defense systems from the front lines to Moscow, the Kerch Strait Bridge, and Valdai (where Russian elites have vacation homes), prioritizing leadership and strategic asset protection over soldier safety. This reallocation is being forced by Ukraine's escalating strikes on Russian logistics, Moscow, and Crimea. Ukraine has created a strategic trap: by targeting these high-value locations, it forces Russia to choose between protecting critical infrastructure or the front lines, but Russia lacks sufficient air defenses to protect both simultaneously. When Russia reinforces Moscow or Crimea, the front lines become exposed; when it protects the front lines, those cities remain vulnerable.

Russia is now trapped in a reactive cycle with no good options. With air defenses withdrawn, Russian soldiers face undersupply, lack of aerial protection, and no incentive to hold positions, creating conditions for mass abandonment even without a full Ukrainian offensive. Ukraine is timing the arrival of Swedish Gripen fighter jets to exploit these vulnerabilities and has shifted from a grinding defensive strategy to actively dictating where and how battles are fought. Unless Russia negotiates surrender, it will likely face compounding military failures as Ukraine maintains momentum and controls the battlefield initiative.
