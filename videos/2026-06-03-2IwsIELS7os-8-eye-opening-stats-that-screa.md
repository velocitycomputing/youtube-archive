## Summary

A financial adviser shares eight statistics challenging the early retirement myths. Key findings include: Americans' healthy life expectancy is only 66.1 years (13 months after standard retirement age), median household net worth for 55–64-year-olds is $365,000 (roughly $200,000 excluding home equity), and the million-dollar retirement target is unrealistic for most Americans. The 80% income replacement rule is flawed—Goldman Sachs research shows equal satisfaction between those spending 50%, 60%, or 70–80% of pre-retirement income. Social Security provides $2,000/month per person (~$48,000 annually for couples), double the senior poverty line. Contrary to the "retirement smile" theory, a JP Morgan study found retiree expenses don't increase later due to healthcare, as other expenses drop faster. About 80% of retirees report happiness after the initial transition.

To act on this: define your ideal retirement lifestyle and assign it a specific budget rather than chasing arbitrary targets. Test-drive this budget now (reduce dining out, cut vacations, downsize housing temporarily). Check your Social Security benefit at ssa.gov and use planning software like Bolden (with AI features for scenario modeling) instead of Excel. Research actual retiree spending via the Bureau of Labor Statistics. If you're concerned about boredom or missing work's structure, recognize that the transition period is temporary—most people adapt quickly and wish they'd retired sooner.
