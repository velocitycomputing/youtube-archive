## Summary

Paul McCartney discusses how "Helter Skelter" was written as a competitive response to The Who and became foundational to heavy metal; the Beatles' initial rejection of Yoko in the studio that later evolved into acceptance; the collaborative creation of "Getting Better All the Time" with John Lennon; how The Beatles consistently innovated first before The Rolling Stones followed (psychedelia, fashion, touring); the breakup's emotional toll and Linda's crucial support; and various songwriting decisions including giving unreleased songs to Peter and Gordon. He also covers the band's organic fashion development, John's "bigger than Jesus" comment contextually, recording while John was accidentally on acid, and his ongoing friendships with Keith Richards.

For creative professionals and teams: competitive energy can fuel better work when channeled constructively (Helter Skelter), but requires honest communication rather than passive-aggressive avoidance—the Beatles succeeded when they actively addressed conflicts instead of festering; personal support during professional crisis is invaluable (learn from Linda's role); generous mentorship—sharing work you won't use with emerging artists—builds unexpected returns; and the best output comes from adapting to unexpected inputs rather than rigid control, as shown by successfully finishing a track while John was accidentally tripping on acid.
