## Summary

The speaker describes Crimea experiencing severe infrastructure collapse—fuel shortages, unreliable electricity and water, depleted shops, suspended summer camps—creating pervasive anxiety among residents about basic survival and future stability. Crimea holds exceptional symbolic importance to Putin's regime compared to other Russian territories because it represents imperial restoration and monarchical legitimacy rather than merely state authority; the Soviet-era mythology surrounding Crimea as a luxury Mediterranean destination ties Russia's cultural identity to ancient Greek heritage. While material hardship alone is unlikely to spark overt political resistance, visible deterioration in Crimea is uniquely corrosive to Putin's regime because it undermines the mythmaking about imperial greatness that legitimizes his rule.

To counter Russian propaganda about Crimea's annexation: even a legitimate referendum wouldn't establish legitimacy without a prior secession step (like a country changing unions requires divorce then remarriage). Concretely, the regime is more vulnerable to Crimea's slow strangulation than to military loss—Ukraine progressively cutting supplies without conquering the territory damages Putin's core narrative of imperial restoration more deeply than similar crises elsewhere would, potentially corroding public acquiescence to his rule despite low likelihood of open rebellion.
