## Summary

Swiss watchmaking, centered in the Jura Mountains' "Watch Valley," produces mechanical timepieces entirely by hand using centuries-old techniques, with master craftsmen like Philippe Dufour spending 2,000+ hours creating single watches that sell for hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars at auction. These luxury brands—including Patek Philippe, Rolex, and Richard Mille—deliberately position watches as appreciating art pieces rather than functional timekeeping devices, featuring "complications" (mechanical flourishes like perpetual calendars and minute repeaters) and precision accurate to within 5 seconds per day. The industry survived Japan's 1970s quartz crisis by pivoting to ultra-high-end positioning, combining Alpine pricing with artificially limited production; recent US tariffs (initially 39%, negotiated down to 15%) further drive up costs.

If you're drawn to Swiss watches, understand that purchasing one is a multi-year journey requiring patience and connections—waitlists routinely exceed a decade, and brands like Patek Philippe screen buyers before selling even at $300,000+ list prices. These watches appreciate as investments over time and can resell at significant markups, but makers like Max Buser (who deliberately caps production at ~400 watches yearly) emphasize that emotional attachment to craftsmanship, not financial return, should be your primary motivation. A mechanical watch today is functionally pointless given smartphones, so any purchase is purely about art, heritage, and personal desire.
