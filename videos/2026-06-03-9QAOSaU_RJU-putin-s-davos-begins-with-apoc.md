## Summary

Putin's St. Petersburg economic forum proceeded amid ongoing Ukrainian drone and missile attacks on Russian soil, creating "apocalyptic scenes" with black smoke plumes, explosions, and internet disruptions visible near the city. The forum traditionally serves three critical functions for Putin's regime: projecting stability and safety to attract foreign investment, demonstrating normalcy to the Russian population, and reinforcing Putin's role as the ultimate arbiter within the regime. The speaker analyzes that Putin's 2022 invasion of Ukraine stemmed partly from his perception—accurate and paranoid—that his power was declining relative to elites, and that he sought to reconcentrate authority by starting the war, though this backfired by forcing further delegation during the conflict. The forum's symbolic importance has diminished as Russia's soft power and international standing have declined, making these attacks a significant perceptual blow to Putin's image as a trustworthy referee.

For Western citizens, the key takeaway is recognizing and resisting the depoliticization pattern evident in Russia, where populations normalize deteriorating conditions by disengaging from politics and claiming powerlessness. The speaker warns explicitly against "copying" Russian-style civic disengagement in Western democracies, cautioning that such depoliticization produces leaders like Trump and Farage. The actionable insight is that citizens retain power only by maintaining political awareness and engagement; without the perception that change is possible through collective action, populations unconsciously enable authoritarian consolidation and deteriorating conditions, just as Russian citizens are normalizing escalating warfare as inevitable weather rather than a political choice.
