## Summary

Ukraine has strategically escalated drone strikes on Russian oil refineries, particularly targeting a Moscow refinery that supplies jet fuel to major airports, triggering severe fuel shortages across Russia as summer demand peaks. These strikes exploit cumulative attrition of Russian air defenses degraded over months of warfare, allowing Ukrainian drone waves to penetrate previously impenetrable airspace. Russia now faces shortages in Crimea (supply-line lockdown), Moscow-St. Petersburg corridor (refinery capacity), and agricultural regions (diesel for harvesting), forcing Russia to choose between defending Moscow or Crimea with limited air defense systems. Ukraine's strategy deliberately targets civilian inconvenience—vacation disruptions, long fuel queues, canceled flights—rather than direct threats to life, which pins political pressure on Putin by undermining his core strategy of shielding the Russian population from war consequences.

Russia is trapped in an escalating downward spiral: accumulated refinery damage prevents rapid repairs, imported fuel from previous partners is depleted, forcing costly China deals where Russia exports crude at thin margins and reimports it refined at net loss. This worsening cycle will repeat annually with greater severity, making each year's fuel crisis worse than the last. For Russians, this means accepting that civilian lifestyle disruptions are now permanent consequences of the war, requiring them to reconsider support for continued conflict. For external observers, while Putin's collapse timing is unpredictable, the directional trend is clear and irreversible—this year's crisis is a preview of accelerating economic pressure in subsequent years.
