## Summary

Jaron Lanier discusses how AI is fundamentally people's work combined into documents, not intelligent creatures—information is physical and energy-intensive. He explores how virtual reality, which he pioneered, has failed to reach its potential because tech companies forced it into existing business models (iPad-like devices, social networks) rather than enabling genuinely new experiences like 3D design or four-dimensional thinking. A major issue is that VR companies with homogeneous engineering teams (mostly white men) didn't test for motion sickness in women and Asian populations, causing product failures. The core problem across all tech is the single dominant business model: "influence generation," where platforms become addictive by constantly activating the brain's fight-or-flight response, turning users paranoid and vain. This model traces back to a 1950 warning by Norbert Wiener about behavior-modification machines. Network effects ensure that low-friction "free information" ideologies actually concentrate power in companies like Meta and Google, not distribute it.

To reclaim better technology: delete your social media accounts, as Lanier explicitly recommends. Understand that "free" platforms aren't free—they're leverage for centralized companies to amass wealth and power. Support and use platforms with different business models (GitHub over 4chan shows platforms can vary in quality). If you have tech influence or work in the industry, recognize that social media algorithms reshape personalities and priorities—self-regulate before regulation is forced. Push for alternative business models beyond influence generation; the recent lawsuit against Meta shows momentum for change. For virtual reality, seek out uses that enhance (not replace) reality, like 3D design work. Most importantly, advocate for regulation and transparency about how algorithms modify behavior, since individual choices alone won't counter network effects.
