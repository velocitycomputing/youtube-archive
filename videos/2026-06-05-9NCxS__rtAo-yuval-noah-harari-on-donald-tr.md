## Summary

Ezra Klein interviews Yuval Noah Harari about whether human progress is driven by cooperation or power—a tension exemplified by the Trump administration's view (via Stephen Miller) that the world runs on force and hierarchy. Harari argues that humans achieved civilization through cooperation, not brute force, and that liberalism's core innovation was recognizing that people from different races, nations, and classes can cooperate through shared interests. This enabled the post-WWII system where nations spent more on healthcare than defense for the first time in history. The conversation explores how nationalism, religion, and shared fictions enable large-scale cooperation; why liberalism weakened by abandoning "fraternity" (national community bonds) while emphasizing equality and liberty alone; and how Israel's current shift toward viewing military domination as supreme represents an abandonment of 2,000 years of Jewish emphasis on learning and intellectual difference.

The actionable insight is that a return to "might makes right" international relations will force all nations to massively militarize, making everyone less safe despite appearing stronger—a trap humanity repeatedly failed at historically. For liberal democracies: reclaim nationalism and national storytelling as allies while maintaining self-correcting mechanisms (elections, independent courts, free press) that treat all political stories as human creations subject to amendment, not divine truths. Personally: acknowledge the fictions that bind your community without pretending they are absolute; witness suffering as a moral baseline even when you believe your side is justified; and consider whether abandoning a culture's core values (Harari's warning about Israel replacing 2,000 years of emphasis on learning with militarism) is worth the security gains.
