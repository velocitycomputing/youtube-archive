## Summary

At Encl restaurant in Sonoma, California, Chef Brian operates with a philosophy of maximizing ingredients and cooking over fire. The kitchen features a "meat chandelier" for dry-aging fish bones, vegetables, and quail to concentrate flavors—vegetables hang for a week or longer, quail for two days minimum. The most elaborate process is the 60-day dry-aged blue fin tuna, aged at 75-85% humidity to break down sinew and develop complex flavors; the kitchen also harvests tuna spinal marrow and "black market tuna jelly" to season rice. Venison legs are dry-aged for two weeks for tartare, while lichen foraged from local trees undergoes a two-day process of pressure cooking, soaking, drying, and high-temperature frying before being dusted with fermented spices (tomato, pea shells, cabbage, leeks, pickled garlic). The historic 1850s building incorporates repurposed materials, and service dishes feature garnishes grilled over live almond-wood embers, including pickled roses, beans, and artichoke confit. Chocolate work involves three-tempering cycles for sunflower seed praline bon bons.

The actionable techniques include: dry-aging proteins (60 days for fish, 2 weeks for venison) at controlled humidity to intensify flavors; utilizing whole animals—spines, marrow, organs—that most kitchens discard; fermenting and pickling vegetables to preserve them year-round with concentrated, complex flavors; ladling hot oil over delicate proteins like quail to crisp skin without explosive moisture release; identifying wild, foraged ingredients (like local lichen) and developing multi-step cooking techniques to unlock their potential; and applying heat-controlled finishing methods (like ladle-frying rather than submersion frying) to achieve texture precision.
