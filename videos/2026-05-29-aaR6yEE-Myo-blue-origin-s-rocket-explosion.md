## Summary

Blue Origin's New Glenn booster exploded during a static test fire on May 29, 2026, at Launch Complex 36 on Florida's Space Coast. The second booster ("Never Tell Me the Odds") was being prepared to launch 48 Amazon Kuiper satellites weighing 30 tons—the first heavy payload for New Glenn. The explosion was catastrophic: tanks ruptured almost instantaneously (not gradually), dispersing over 1,000 tons of liquid oxygen and methane that burned with at least 1 kiloton yield, producing a mushroom cloud visible 50 miles away in Orlando. Analysis of the footage shows a well-defined pressure wave and remarkably fast propagation from ignition to total disintegration in seconds. No one was injured. The blast destroyed ground infrastructure including one lightning tower, the transporter erector, and possibly the other booster in the assembly building. Scott Manley emphasized this was detonation-like rather than a burn-then-explosion scenario.

The incident cascades across the entire launch industry. Blue Origin faces a likely 6+ month rebuild of Slick 36 (SpaceX took over a year to restore their damaged pad); Amazon loses critical New Glenn launches needed to deploy their constellation and meet frequency-licensing deadlines; other customers (AS Space Mobile, NASA's Artemis lunar program) must find alternative providers with limited capacity (Falcon 9 has one East Coast site, Vulcan is grounded, Ariane 6 is slow); and the Blue Moon lander—dependent exclusively on New Glenn—won't meet Artemis 3's 2028 timeline. Watch for: Blue Origin's investigation findings, revised launch schedule announcements, Amazon pivoting launches to Falcon 9 or other providers, and impacts to NASA's Artemis lunar landing plans.
